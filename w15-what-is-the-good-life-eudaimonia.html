📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A good knife cuts well. A good eye sees well. What does a good human being do well?
Aristotle was Plato's student for twenty years, and his answer to the question of the good life is both indebted to Plato and sharply different from him: concrete, practical, interested in the actual activities of a fully developed human being living among others, rather than the soul's relationship to eternal Forms. This lesson introduces eudaimonia, usually translated "happiness" but better rendered "flourishing," and the Function Argument Aristotle uses to defend it.

📚 PART 1 · WHY PHILOSOPHY WENT BACK TO ARISTOTLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Having studied Mill and Kant, do you feel either theory fully captured what it means to be a genuinely good person, or did something feel missing? (No wrong answer.)

In the second half of the twentieth century, a number of moral philosophers grew dissatisfied with the two modern traditions this module has just examined. In a landmark 1958 essay, the philosopher G.E.M. Anscombe argued that both utilitarianism and Kantian deontology had quietly dropped something essential: an account of what kind of person a moral agent needs to become. Utilitarianism asks only what produces the best outcome; Kantian ethics asks only what duty or universal rule an action follows. Neither, Anscombe and philosophers who followed her, including Philippa Foot and later Alasdair MacIntyre, argued, has much to say about character: about the habits, dispositions, and practical judgment a person actually needs in order to live well, as opposed to calculating correctly or applying the right formula in a single moment of decision. These philosophers called for a return to a much older way of asking the ethical question, one that had been largely set aside since the Enlightenment: not "what rule should I follow" or "what produces the most good," but "what does it mean to be a good person, and how does someone become one?" This is virtue ethics, and its revival sent moral philosophy back to where it had started twenty-two centuries earlier: Aristotle.

Aristotle came to Athens at seventeen and studied at Plato's Academy for twenty years. Where Plato located the good life in the soul's relationship to eternal, unchanging Forms, Aristotle located it in the concrete activities of a fully developed human being living in community with others. His major ethical work, the Nicomachean Ethics, opens with a question that sounds simple but is surprisingly hard to answer: what is the highest good for human beings, the thing we are ultimately aiming at?

📚 PART 2 · EUDAIMONIA: MORE THAN HAPPINESS

Every action and pursuit seems to aim at some good; some goods are pursued for their own sake, others for the sake of something further. That chain of purposes cannot go on forever; there must be something at the end that we want for its own sake and nothing beyond it, the highest good. Aristotle notes that almost everyone agrees this is happiness, but the agreement collapses the moment people try to say what happiness actually is.

The Greek word is eudaimonia, usually translated "happiness" but more accurately "flourishing" or "living and doing well." Modern happiness typically names a feeling, an emotional state of contentment. Eudaimonia is not primarily a feeling; it is an activity, a way of living. Aristotle defines it as "activity of the soul in accordance with virtue, and if there are more than one virtue, in accordance with the best and most complete" (NE I.7, 1098a17–18). A person can feel happy in the short term through passive pleasure or numbing entertainment while not flourishing at all; Aristotle is interested in what a life looks like when it is genuinely going well, not in a subjective feeling-state.

📚 PART 3 · THE FUNCTION ARGUMENT

Aristotle's central argument for what eudaimonia consists in is the Function Argument. Every kind of thing has a characteristic activity that defines its excellence: a good knife cuts well, a good eye sees well, a good musician plays excellently. What is the distinctive function of a human being? Plants share nutrition and growth with us; animals share perception and locomotion. What is uniquely human is the capacity for rational activity, deliberating about what is good and governing oneself accordingly. Therefore, the good for human beings is the excellent exercise of this rational capacity.

⚡ TRY IT · WHAT DOES THE FUNCTION ARGUMENT ESTABLISH?
Evaluate each claim about what Aristotle's Function Argument does or does not establish.
1. "Since eudaimonia is defined by rational activity, pleasure must be irrelevant to the good life."
2. "Aristotle thinks a person in extreme poverty or serious illness cannot fully flourish, however virtuous they are."

This last point distinguishes Aristotle sharply from Kant: for Kant, the good will has worth regardless of external circumstance, rich or poor, healthy or ill. For Aristotle, human flourishing is irreducibly embedded in the natural and social world. What makes rational activity excellent is virtue (arete), and Aristotle's most consequential claim about virtue, that we become virtuous by practicing virtuous action rather than merely by knowing what virtue is, is the subject of Lesson 15b.
