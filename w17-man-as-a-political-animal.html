📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
"He who is unable to live in society... must be either a beast or a god." Which are you?
Hobbes presented human beings in their natural state as isolated and at war. Aristotle, writing two thousand years earlier in the Politics, begins from the opposite premise: "man is by nature a political animal." This lesson unpacks what Aristotle means by nature, and why he thinks political community completes rather than constrains human nature.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain what "natural" means for Aristotle in a teleological sense, distinct from a historical claim.
2
I will be able to explain the argument from logos for why man is more political than bees or other social animals.
3
I will be able to explain what Aristotle means by saying the polis is "prior to the individual."

Aristotle, Politics I.2
📚 PART 1 · WHAT "NATURAL" MEANS FOR ARISTOTLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Does it feel true to you that a person living entirely alone, with no community at all, would be missing something essential to being fully human? (No wrong answer.)

When Aristotle says the polis is "a creation of nature" and man is "by nature a political animal" (Politics I.2, 1253a2–3), he is not making a historical claim about how cities arose. He is making a teleological claim. For Aristotle, the nature of a thing is its telos, its characteristic end. The nature of an acorn is to become an oak; a "natural eye" is a healthy, functioning eye. When Aristotle says man is by nature political, he means a fully developed human being is one who lives in political community. A human being outside political community is not in a purer state; they are in a diminished one, like an acorn that never became an oak.

This is the exact opposite of Hobbes's assumption. For Hobbes, the pre-political isolated individual is the natural baseline, and political community is artificial, built on top of it. For Aristotle, political community is part of the natural baseline itself, where human nature arrives when it is functioning correctly.

📚 PART 2 · SPEECH, REASON, AND THE GOOD

Aristotle's argument turns on logos, a word meaning both speech and reason. Other animals have voice (phōnē): they signal pleasure, pain, and danger. Human beings alone have logos: the capacity to use language to reason about and discuss good and bad, just and unjust. "Man is the only animal who has the gift of speech... Speech is designed to indicate the advantageous and the harmful, and therefore also the right and the wrong... And the association of living beings who have this sense makes a family and a polis." (Politics I.2, 1253a7–18) A beehive is not a polis, because bees cannot ask whether their arrangement is just. Human beings can ask this question, and that capacity is what makes them political animals rather than merely social ones.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL · PHŌNĒ OR LOGOS?
Phōnē
(voice)
Shared with other animals: signals pleasure, pain, and danger. A dog's bark warns of a threat, but it cannot ask whether the threat is being treated justly.
Logos
(speech-reason)
Distinctively human: the capacity to reason about and discuss what is good and bad, just and unjust. This is what makes a genuine polis, not just a herd, possible.
📚 PART 3 · THE SOLITARY MAN, AND THE POLIS AS PRIOR

Aristotle considers what it would mean to live entirely outside political community: "He who is unable to live in society, or who has no need because he is sufficient for himself, must be either a beast or a god; he is no part of a polis." (Politics I.2, 1253a27–30) A beast lacks logos; a god needs no community because it is fully self-sufficient. Human beings are neither: they are dependent, rational creatures whose full development requires political community.

Aristotle also claims the polis is prior to the individual, not in time, but in nature and completion: "the individual, when isolated, is not self-sufficing; and therefore he is like a part in relation to the whole." (Politics I.2, 1253a25–27) A hand separated from the body is no longer really a hand, since it cannot perform its function; a human being separated from political community cannot exercise the distinctively human capacities for reasoned speech and shared deliberation about the good. This is not a claim that individuals have no rights against the state; it is a claim about the conditions human flourishing actually requires.
