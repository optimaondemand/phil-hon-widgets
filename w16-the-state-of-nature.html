📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
"Solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." One sentence that has echoed through political philosophy for 375 years.
Chapter 13 of Leviathan asks us to imagine human beings with no laws, no courts, no police, no government at all. Hobbes's answer is not that people would be monsters. It is that the structural logic of human existence without authority produces conflict as a predictable outcome, whatever anyone's character.

▶
Thomas Hobbes, Leviathan, Chapters 13–14
📚 PART 1 · NATURAL EQUALITY AND THE WAR OF ALL AGAINST ALL
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Do you think a person's physical strength or intelligence makes them meaningfully safe from being harmed by someone weaker or less clever? (No wrong answer.)

Hobbes begins with human equality, meaning rough equality of power, not moral equality. No one is so strong they cannot be killed by a coalition of weaker people; no one so clever they cannot be outwitted. "The difference between man and man is not so considerable as that one man can thereupon claim to himself any benefit to which another may not pretend as well as he." (Leviathan, ch. 13) From equality of ability comes equality of hope in attaining one's goals, and from equality of hope comes conflict.

From this, Hobbes concludes that without a common power to overawe everyone, human beings are in a state of war, not a series of battles but a persistent condition of insecurity: "such a war as is of every man against every man." (Leviathan, ch. 13) In this condition there is no farming, no trade, no arts, no letters, no society, because all productive activity requires security that anything produced might not simply be taken by force.

📚 PART 2 · THREE CAUSES OF QUARREL

Hobbes identifies three sources of conflict. Competition: if two people want the same scarce thing, they become enemies. Diffidence, mutual distrust: even without a current dispute, I have reason to fear you might attack me later, so the rational move is to strike first, and you, reasoning the same way about me, have the same incentive. Glory: some people attack not for resources or safety but to demonstrate power and be feared. "First, competition; secondly, diffidence; thirdly, glory." (Leviathan, ch. 13)

⚡ TRY IT · WHICH CAUSE OF QUARREL?
Identify which of Hobbes's three causes best explains each scenario.
1. A person attacks a neighbor, not because the neighbor has done anything yet, but because the neighbor might attack first if given the chance
2. A person attacks a stranger for no reason other than to make sure everyone in the area fears and respects them
📚 PART 3 · THE RIGHT OF NATURE AND THE LAWS OF NATURE

In the state of nature, each person has a right of nature (jus naturale): an unlimited right to use whatever means they judge necessary to preserve their own life, including preemptive violence. But reason also generates laws of nature (lex naturalis), rational prescriptions for surviving and prospering. The first: seek peace when there is hope of it, and use the advantages of war only when peace is unavailable. The second follows: when others are willing, lay down your unlimited right to all things and accept mutual restraint, in other words, enter a social contract and submit to a common authority.

Hobbes is careful to note the state of nature is a thought experiment, not necessarily a historical claim, though he points to contemporary examples: indigenous peoples as travelers described them, and the permanent mutual suspicion between states with no common authority above them. The point is not to describe the past but to explain the present: political authority is justified because life without it would be terrible, and we accept constraints on our freedom because the alternative is worse.
