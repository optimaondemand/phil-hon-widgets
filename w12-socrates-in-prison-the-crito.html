📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Your oldest friend has bribed the guards. The boat is arranged. All you have to do is walk out. Would it be right?
Waiting in an Athenian prison for his execution, Socrates receives a visit before dawn from Crito, an old friend with a plan to help him escape. Plato's Crito is the record of the conversation that follows: not "can I escape" but "would it be right to escape." Module 4 opens here because the question the Crito raises — what does justice actually demand of us — is the question the whole module pursues, through Plato, Mill, Kant, and Aristotle in turn.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain the arguments Crito actually makes for escape, and why the claim that the trial was unjust is the one that bites hardest.
2
I will be able to state Socrates's methodological principle that majority opinion does not determine what is right, and his refusal to answer one wrong with another.
3
I will be able to explain the personified Laws' argument from agreement, and why Socrates ultimately refuses to leave Athens.

📚 PART 1 · THE PROBLEM CRITO BRINGS
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
If a legal system genuinely wronged you, would you feel you still owed it obedience? (No wrong answer.)

Crito's proposal is not simple self-interest; he has reasons. First, he says he will suffer if Socrates dies, and people who do not understand what happened will think Socrates's friends could have saved him and chose not to. Second, the money and the arrangements are no obstacle: he has enough, and friends in Thessaly are ready to take Socrates in. Third, and most pointed: the trial itself was unjust, manipulated by Anytus and Meletus. Why, Crito asks, should Socrates defer to a verdict that wronged him?

Socrates's response opens with method before substance. What the majority thinks does not determine what is right; only careful reasoning does. He has held to this before, but never with such urgency: "I am not only now but always a man who follows nothing but the reasoning which on reflection seems best to me" (Crito 46b). From there he restates the principle he has always lived by: never do wrong in return for wrong, and never harm anyone, even someone who has harmed you. If it was right before the trial, it is right now. So the real question is not whether the jury acted unjustly — it did — but whether escaping would itself be an act of injustice toward Athens.

📚 PART 2 · THE LAWS SPEAK

Socrates now performs one of the most striking moves in all of Plato: he imagines the Laws of Athens themselves appearing to demand an account. Their first point is structural. A legal system survives only if its verdicts are enforced; if private individuals may set aside judgments they consider unjust, the whole framework collapses. "Do you think it possible for a city not to be destroyed if the verdicts of its courts have no force but are nullified and set at naught by private individuals?" (Crito 50b)

The Laws press further with an argument from agreement. Socrates has not merely lived under their protection; his parents' marriage, his birth, his education, seventy years of residence he never chose to leave, all occurred under their authority. To stay in a city, knowing its laws, and enjoy its benefits is to enter an implicit agreement to accept its legal processes, including adverse verdicts. If you disagree with a judgment, the proper channel is persuasion, not unilateral defiance: "You must either persuade it or obey its orders… whether blows or bonds" (Crito 51b–c). This claim is strong, and Socrates does not pretend it settles every case — oppressive regimes and unappealable verdicts raise real questions his argument does not fully answer. But his own city offered him actual channels for appeal and persuasion; the injustice done to him was the work of his accusers and jurors, not a defect in the legal system as such.

📚 PART 3 · WHY NOT THESSALY?

The Laws also turn Crito's practical plan on its head. In Thessaly, Socrates would live as a fugitive dependent on strangers, cut off from his city and the friends of seventy years of philosophical conversation, and he would have betrayed the very teaching that the soul matters more than survival. Worse, he would be showing his own children that self-preservation outranks justice whenever the cost gets high enough. The Laws close by appealing to Socrates's own deepest convictions: the injustice done to him was real, but it was done by men, not by the Laws themselves, and escaping now would not correct that injustice; it would only add a second one.

Socrates tells Crito he hears the Laws' argument "like music," and no other reasoning can overcome it. He will stay. But the Crito's philosophical work is not to close the question of justice; it is to open it. Socrates has shown that justice is not self-interest, that majority opinion does not settle what is right, and that legal obligation can be real even when a particular verdict is unjust. What he has not shown is what justice actually is. That is the question Plato's Republic takes up next, and the question the rest of Module 4 pursues across three very different answers: Mill's utilitarian answer, Kant's answer from duty, and Aristotle's answer from virtue and flourishing.
