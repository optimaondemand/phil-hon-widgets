📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Is something good because God loves it, or does God love it because it is good?
Lesson 2b ended with an unresolved question, briefly raised: Socrates showed that Euthyphro's third definition of piety, what all the gods love, tells us something that accompanies piety, an attribute, rather than piety's essence. But in pressing that point, Socrates asked a question so sharp it has never fully gone away: “Is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or is it pious because it is loved by the gods?” (Euthyphro, 10a) This lesson works through that question in full.

📚 PART 1 · THE TWO HORNS
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
If God commanded something cruel, would it become good simply because God commanded it? (No wrong answer; just notice your instinct before you read on.)

A dilemma, from the Greek for “two propositions,” is a situation in which you are forced to choose between two options, and both lead somewhere undesirable. The Euthyphro Dilemma has two such options, called horns.

HORN ONE
The good is good independently of God. The gods recognize goodness; they do not create it. Morality is something God discovers, not invents.
HORN TWO
The good is whatever God approves. Goodness has no nature apart from God's will. There is no standard above God.

These two horns appear to exhaust the possibilities: either goodness is independent of God, or it is not, and there seems to be no room for anything in between, since Horn Two is simply the denial of Horn One. It looks like a closed either/or.

But look closely at what the two horns assume in common: both treat God and goodness as two separate things that must stand in some relationship, with one explaining the other. Either goodness comes first and God's love follows it, or God's will comes first and goodness follows that. If that shared assumption could be questioned, a third possibility might open up: not a third answer to “which comes first,” but a denial that the two need to be ranked at all. Whether that possibility actually works is what Part 3 of this lesson will examine. For now, hold both horns in mind, since each carries a real cost.

📚 PART 2 · WHY EACH HORN IS UNATTRACTIVE

The Problem with Horn One. If goodness is independent of God, if piety exists as a standard that God recognizes but did not create, then God seems to become philosophically unnecessary, at least in ethics. We could investigate what is good without any reference to God, since those standards exist on their own. It also seems to diminish God: a being bound by standards above and outside himself does not seem fully sovereign. Some have found this picture of God deeply unsatisfying. A God who answers to something higher than himself begins to look less like the author of all things and more like a very wise creature among other creatures.

The Problem with Horn Two. This avoids the first problem by making God the source of goodness, but creates a different and arguably worse difficulty. If goodness just is whatever God commands, then God's commands are not themselves good for any reason; they simply are what they are. If God had commanded cruelty, cruelty would be good. This is sometimes called the problem of divine arbitrariness. Most religious believers, and most philosophers in the Western tradition, find it deeply uncomfortable. We do not typically think torturing children would be good if God commanded it; we think it would remain wrong regardless. That intuition suggests we hold something fixed about good and evil that does not bend simply because someone, even God, commands otherwise.

📚 PART 3 · HOW THE PHILOSOPHER MIGHT RESPOND

The dilemma has generated centuries of reflection. Three responses are worth examining.

The Third Option: God's Nature as the Standard. The most influential response, developed by Augustine and Aquinas, is that the dilemma presents a false choice. Goodness is neither independent of God nor arbitrarily decreed by God, because goodness just is God's own nature. God does not consult an external standard, nor invent goodness by fiat. He simply is good, essentially and necessarily. This is the possibility Part 1 gestured toward: it denies that God and goodness are two separate things needing to be ranked. The question “is it good because God loves it, or does God love it because it is good?” dissolves, because there are not two separate things being compared. This preserves both divine sovereignty and the non-arbitrariness of goodness. Whether it fully succeeds is debated; some argue it only relocates the dilemma rather than dissolving it. But it is a serious, historically important answer.

Accepting Horn One. Some philosophers, possibly including Plato himself, accept the first horn without embarrassment. The Form of the Good, in Plato's Republic, is the highest object of philosophical knowledge, standing above even the gods; for Plato this is not a weakness but the whole point.

Accepting Horn Two, with Qualifications. Other thinkers accept the second horn while limiting its troubling implications: if God is essentially rational and loving, his commands will reflect those attributes reliably, so cruelty is never actually commanded, not because an external standard forbids it, but because commanding cruelty would contradict what God is. This response is close to the third option and faces similar questions about whether it truly escapes the dilemma.

📚 PART 4 · THE DILEMMA'S LASTING FORCE

What makes the Euthyphro Dilemma genuinely difficult, and genuinely worth studying, is that it does not arise from a trick or a technicality. It arises from two things most thoughtful people, across very different traditions, believe: that God is the source of all things, and that morality is not arbitrary. If both are true, we need an account of how they fit together, and the dilemma shows that fitting them together is harder than it looks.

Socrates asked the question in the middle of a conversation about a legal case. Euthyphro, about to do something drastic in the name of piety, could not answer it. He excused himself and left. The question has outlasted both of them. That is the mark of a genuine philosophical problem: not that it cannot be answered, but that every attempt to answer it demands more careful thought than we initially supposed.

💪 HOLD ONTO THIS

Notice how careful Socrates is about the shape of his own argument. He does not just assert that goodness and God's will can't be pulled apart cleanly; he shows it, by testing every option honestly, including options he might have wanted to reject immediately. That carefulness is worth imitating whenever you are the one holding the dilemma.
