📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Light hits your retina. Neurons fire. Where does the felt experience of reading come from?
The mind-body problem, explaining the relationship between the physical processes of the brain and the subjective experience of a conscious mind, is one of the oldest and hardest problems in philosophy. This week's reading, Descartes' Meditations on First Philosophy, Meditation VI, gives one of the most influential answers ever offered, roughly two thousand years after Plato's own answer in the Phaedo.

2
I will be able to explain the cogito and why it establishes the self as a thinking thing, not a body.
3
I will be able to compare Descartes's route to dualism with Plato's route in the Phaedo.

📚 PART 1 · WHO IS DESCARTES?
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
If you tried to doubt absolutely everything you believe, is there anything you think would survive? (No wrong answer.)

René Descartes (1596-1650) is often called the father of modern philosophy, for setting philosophy on a new trajectory away from inherited authority and toward individual reason as the foundation of knowledge. He lived at the beginning of the Scientific Revolution, as Galileo's proof that the earth moves around the sun overturned centuries of Church teaching and Aristotelian science. Descartes wanted to build something new: a philosophy as certain as mathematics, built from the ground up on foundations that could not be doubted. His method was radical: doubt everything that could possibly be doubted, and see what survives.

📚 PART 2 · THE METHOD OF DOUBT

Can he doubt the external world exists? Yes, he might be dreaming. Can he doubt his body exists? Yes, a disembodied mind could have all the same experiences. Can he doubt the truths of mathematics? With effort, even two plus two might be the implant of a deceiving demon. But there is one thing he cannot doubt: that he is doubting. Doubt is a kind of thought. If he is thinking, he must exist. This is the famous cogito: "I think, therefore I am." (Meditations, II)

Notice what this establishes: Descartes exists as a thinking thing, a mind, not as a body. His first certain knowledge of himself is as a mind, not a body. The question then becomes: what is the relationship between this thinking thing and the physical body he seems to inhabit?

⚡ TRY IT · SURVIVES THE DOUBT, OR NOT?
For each claim, decide whether Descartes's method of doubt can eliminate it.
1. "My body exists and I am sitting in a chair right now."
2. "I am currently thinking, whatever else might be false."
📚 PART 3 · A NEW VERSION OF AN OLD QUESTION

If this sounds familiar, it should. In the Phaedo, Plato argued the soul is entirely different from the body, immaterial, unchanging, related to the eternal Forms rather than to the flux of the physical world. Descartes arrives at a similar destination by a very different road. Plato's route was metaphysical: the soul belongs to the realm of Forms. Descartes's route is epistemological: he knows he is a mind before he knows he has a body, so mind and body must be distinct things. Both end up with a picture of the human being as a composite of two radically different kinds of thing, called dualism.

But here is the problem this picture creates. If mind and body are two completely different kinds of substance, how do they interact? How does a thought cause your hand to move? This is the interaction problem, the heart of the mind-body problem, and Lesson 10b examines Descartes's own attempted answer, and why it has never fully satisfied philosophers.
