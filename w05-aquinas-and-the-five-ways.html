📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Five different roads. One claimed destination.
Thomas Aquinas died in 1274 at forty-nine, having produced more systematic philosophical theology than most scholars produce in twice the years. The section you are reading, Question 2, Article 3 of the Summa Theologica, contains his five arguments for God's existence, known as the Five Ways (quinque viae). They are among the most analyzed and contested arguments in Western philosophy, and this lesson walks through each of them carefully.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to reconstruct each of the Five Ways from its starting point to its conclusion.
2
I will be able to explain Aquinas's scholastic method: objections, sed contra, corpus, replies.
3
I will be able to state precisely what the Five Ways do and do not establish about God's nature.

📚 PART 1 · HOW TO READ THE SUMMA
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
When you write an argumentative essay, do you usually address the strongest objection to your view, or mostly just build your own case? (No wrong answer.)

Each Article in the Summa takes the form of a debate. It begins with the Objections, the strongest arguments against the position Aquinas will defend. Then the Sed contra, a brief statement of the opposing view. Then the Corpus, where Aquinas gives his own argument. Finally, Replies to Objections, addressing each objection stated at the beginning. When you read Article 3, notice that Aquinas opens by presenting arguments that God does not exist, including the argument from evil and the argument from natural explanation. He takes these seriously and answers them at the end. This structure is one of the things that makes Aquinas worth reading as a philosopher, not merely as a theologian.

📚 PART 2 · THE FIVE WAYS

The First Way (Motion). Things change: cold iron becomes hot, a seed becomes a tree. Whatever is moved is moved by another; nothing can bring itself from potentiality to actuality, since it would already need the actuality it is trying to acquire. This chain of movers cannot regress infinitely, or there would be no first mover and therefore no motion at all. But motion exists. Therefore there is a first mover, moved by no other: "this everyone understands to be God."

The Second Way (Causation). Nothing is the efficient cause of itself. A chain of causes cannot regress infinitely, or there would be no first cause, and consequently no ultimate or intermediate effects either. Therefore there is a first efficient cause, caused by nothing prior. Aquinas is not claiming the universe had a beginning in time here; that is a separate question. He is arguing that even an eternal universe needs a causal first, or the whole chain of causation is unintelligible.

The Third Way (Contingency). Things in the world are contingent: they can exist or not exist. If everything were contingent, it is possible for everything to not exist simultaneously, and if there were ever genuinely nothing, nothing could come from it. But there is something now. Therefore not everything is contingent; something exists necessarily, and if that necessity is not borrowed from another, it must belong to a being that is necessary in itself.

The Fourth Way (Degrees of Perfection). We compare things as more or less good, true, or real, which implies a maximum by which all others are measured. Aquinas borrows the principle that the maximum in any genus causes all others in that genus. The maximum in being is therefore the cause of being and goodness in everything else. This is the Way most contested by modern philosophers, since that borrowed principle is not self-evident outside Aristotelian metaphysics.

The Fifth Way (Governance). Natural bodies without intelligence, like acorns or planets, act consistently toward ends. Non-intelligent things cannot direct themselves toward an end, any more than an arrow can aim itself. Therefore there must be an intelligent being by whom all natural things are directed to their end. This Way connects most directly to the Design Argument you will read in Lesson 5c.

⚡ TRY IT · MATCH THE WAY TO ITS STARTING POINT
Each Way begins from a different observable feature of the world. Match the starting point to the correct Way.
1. We routinely compare things as more or less good, true, or real
2. Acorns reliably become oaks, not pines, without any intelligence directing them
3. Organisms are born and die; stars form and collapse; things that exist now might not have existed at all
📚 PART 3 · AQUINAS'S METHOD, AND WHAT THE WAYS LEAVE OPEN

Aquinas argues not that any one Way is conclusive alone, but that together they converge on the same conclusion from different starting points: motion, causation, contingency, gradation, and purpose all point to a first, uncaused, necessary being. Each argument identifies a feature of the world that seems to require an explanation the world itself cannot supply.

💡 A PHILOSOPHICALLY THIN CONCLUSION
The Five Ways do not establish that God is personal, loves human beings, responds to prayer, or has communicated with any particular people in history. Those are claims of revealed theology. What the Five Ways establish, if they succeed, is that something exists that is unmoved, uncaused, necessary, maximally perfect, and supremely intelligent. Whether that something is the God of Abraham is a further question the arguments alone do not settle.

These are the arguments you will work with in your capstone essay. Lesson 5c turns from Aquinas to another thinker who believed, eight centuries later, that the natural world testified to God, in an argument Aquinas would not have recognized as his own.
