📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Four philosophers, four answers to the same question. Which one is right, and can they all be a little bit right at once?
This closing lesson of Module 4 examines the four cardinal virtues, courage, temperance, justice, and practical wisdom, the excellences on which Aristotle thinks a good character turns. It then draws together everything the module has covered: Plato's just soul, Mill's happiness, Kant's good will, and Aristotle's eudaimonia, four genuinely different answers to a single question that Glaucon first forced philosophy to take seriously.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to define and distinguish the four cardinal virtues: courage, temperance, justice, and practical wisdom.
2
I will be able to explain how Aristotle's account of justice differs from Plato's and Kant's.
3
I will be able to summarize and compare Plato's, Mill's, Kant's, and Aristotle's answers to Glaucon's challenge.

📚 PART 1 · THE FOUR CARDINAL VIRTUES
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Of the four cardinal virtues, courage, temperance, justice, and practical wisdom, which do you think is hardest for you personally to practice consistently? (No wrong answer.)

The word "cardinal" comes from the Latin cardo, hinge: these are the virtues on which the good life turns. Courage (andreia) is the mean between cowardice and recklessness; the courageous person feels real fear at real danger but acts rightly despite it, judging the thing pursued worth the risk. Temperance (sophrosyne) is the moderate, appropriate enjoyment of bodily pleasure; its goal is not suppressing appetite but educating it, so the temperate person naturally wants the right things in the right amount rather than fighting their own desires. Practical wisdom (phronesis), covered in Lesson 15b, is the intellectual virtue that perceives what each situation requires and unifies the others into a single, coherent character.

📚 PART 2 · JUSTICE, ONE MORE TIME

Justice (dikaiosyne) occupies a special place because it is the virtue most directly about our treatment of others, giving people what they are due, distributing goods and burdens fairly, rectifying wrongs. The connection back to Glaucon is direct: for Aristotle, a person who acts unjustly while appearing just has not "gotten away with" anything real. They have failed to develop one of the fundamental excellences of human life, whatever they materially gain.

⚡ TRY IT · WHOSE ACCOUNT OF JUSTICE IS THIS?
Match each account of justice to the philosopher who holds it.
1. Justice is an excellence of character, a settled disposition to give others what they are due and to take justice's demands seriously even at a cost
2. Justice is each part of the soul, reason, spirit, and appetite, performing its proper function in a harmonious internal order
📚 PART 3 · FOUR TRADITIONS, ONE QUESTION
ThinkerThe good life consists in…Answer to Glaucon
PlatoThe just soul: reason governing spirit and appetiteInjustice disorders the soul, whatever anyone thinks of you
MillHappiness: the active enjoyment of higher pleasuresThe just life produces more genuine happiness, on the whole
KantThe good will: acting from duty, respecting persons as endsInjustice fails to live in accordance with your own rational nature
AristotleEudaimonia: excellent activity across a complete lifeInjustice is a diminished, unflourishing way of being human

These traditions are not simply competitors to be ranked and discarded. Each captures something real: consequences matter, as Mill insists; persons have dignity that resists calculation, as Kant insists; character and habituation matter, as Aristotle insists; and the soul's own inner order matters, as Plato insists. The best ethical reasoning is not sectarian; it draws on all four, and exercises the kind of judgment Aristotle called practical wisdom to weigh them in a given case.

The Module 4 Major Assignment asks you to enter this conversation directly: choose one account, defend it as the most persuasive answer to what makes a human life genuinely good, and confront the strongest objection to it.
