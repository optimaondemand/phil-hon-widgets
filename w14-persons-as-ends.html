📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A murderer asks you where your friend is hiding. Kant seems to say: tell the truth anyway. Is that morality, or is it madness?
Kant's most influential and most challenging claim is that every person has dignity: intrinsic worth that cannot be reduced to usefulness or traded off against aggregate benefit. This idea underlies modern human rights discourse and medical ethics, and it also generates the most famous and counterintuitive objection to Kant's entire framework.

📚 PART 1 · PRICE AND DIGNITY
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Can a person's worth ever be fully captured by a number, a salary, a market value, a social media following? (No wrong answer.)

Kant distinguishes two kinds of value. Things with price can be exchanged, compared, replaced; a car, a piece of furniture, even some skills, all have a price the market will pay. Things with dignity stand above exchange altogether; their worth is incomparable, not greater than priced things but of a fundamentally different kind. For Kant, rational agency, the capacity to govern oneself by principles of reason, is the source of dignity, and every being capable of moral reasoning has it. Treating a person as merely a tool, as if their rationality and capacity for consent were irrelevant, mistakes a being with dignity for something with only price.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL · APPLICATIONS OF THE HUMANITY FORMULA
Deception
Manipulating someone's beliefs without their consent bypasses their capacity to evaluate evidence and decide for themselves, even if the deception is well-intentioned.
Coercion
Threatening or forcing someone bypasses persuasion by reasons entirely, treating them as a mechanism to be operated rather than a person to be reasoned with.
Manipulation
Exploiting someone's psychological vulnerabilities to engineer their "choice" still overrides their genuine rational deliberation, even without force.
📚 PART 2 · THE MURDERER AT THE DOOR

The most famous objection to Kant's framework: a murderer asks whether your friend, hiding inside, is home. Telling the truth means your friend is found and killed. Most people's instinct is that of course you may lie, and should. Yet Kant, in a late essay, appeared to argue that lying is wrong even here, and that moral responsibility for the death would rest entirely with the murderer.

Several responses are worth weighing. Kant may simply have been wrong in that later essay in ways that do not follow from the Groundwork itself, which does not fully address genuine conflicts between duties. Alternatively, the universalizability test may give a different verdict than Kant reached: the maxim "lie when telling the truth would let a murderer harm an innocent person" does not obviously destroy the practice of truthful communication the way routine convenience-lying does, so it might pass where ordinary lying fails. Most importantly, the murderer-at-the-door case, however troubling, does not undermine the central insight that persons have dignity and must not be used merely as means; conflating a hard edge case with the theory's core claim obscures more than it reveals.

📚 PART 3 · KANT, PLATO, AND MILL ON GLAUCON

Three thinkers, three answers to why you should be just. Plato: because justice is the right ordering of the soul, and a just soul is a healthy one; the unjust person has damaged something internal. Mill: because the just life, all things considered, produces more happiness, especially more of the higher pleasures. Kant: because you are a rational being, and to act unjustly is to fail to govern yourself by principles any rational being can recognize and endorse, a failure to be what you are. Each answer captures something the others miss, and each faces a real difficulty: for Kant, the worry is whether anyone is actually motivated by duty alone, and whether that is even what morality should ask of us. Aristotle's answer, taken up next week, suggests Kant's sharp separation of duty from desire misses something essential about what it is to be genuinely virtuous.

Even readers who ultimately reject Kant's framework as too rigid must grapple with the dignity intuition driving it: any adequate moral theory must explain why persons cannot simply be used as instruments for other ends, and it must do so in terms that do not reduce to utilitarian arithmetic.
