📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A God small enough to argue about, or a God too incomprehensible to reason with. Which is worse?
By the end of Part II, the Dialogues has reached genuine tension. Part IV introduces an unexpected line of attack, not from the skeptical Philo, but from the orthodox Demea. The problem Demea raises is different in character from Philo's objections, but strikes at the same conclusion by a different route, and for a moment the two critics find themselves on the same side.

📚 PART 1 · THE CHARGE OF ANTHROPOMORPHISM
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Would you rather worship a God you could fully understand, or one whose nature is genuinely beyond human comprehension? (No wrong answer.)

Cleanthes' argument concludes that the Author of Nature "is somewhat similar to the mind of man, though possessed of much larger faculties." Demea finds this scandalous. The God of traditional theology is not a very-large version of a human mind: God does not deliberate over options or think in the sequential way human thought operates. God is simple, eternal, incomprehensible, radically different in kind, not merely in degree. "Incomprehensibility and obscurity are, in some respects, the most sacred attributes of the Deity." (Dialogues, Part IV) By making God's mind similar in kind to a human mind, Demea argues, Cleanthes has made God a glorified creature rather than the transcendent being theology requires.

Cleanthes refuses to be moved. If God is genuinely incomprehensible, if we can say nothing positive about what God is like, then we have lost the ground needed to defend religion at all. If "God" refers to something with no knowable properties, the claim "God exists" becomes empty and cannot be evaluated, defended, or distinguished from atheism. A God who cannot be described at all cannot be reasoned about.

📚 PART 2 · THE REGRESS OF MIND

Philo takes advantage of this new conflict to press a further argument. Suppose we grant Cleanthes' conclusion: the order of the universe is produced by a mind. A mind, at least any mind we have experience of, is itself an ordered thing: it contains ideas, memories, reasoning capacities, and by Cleanthes' own logic, wherever we find order, we should infer a cause proportioned to it. So an intelligent, planning divine mind, which must be ordered, requires an explanation too. "A mental world… requires a cause as much as does a material world… and if similar in its arrangement, must require a similar cause." (Dialogues, Part IV)

This does not prove God does not exist. It identifies an apparent inconsistency: if the principle "order requires an intelligent cause" is the basis for inferring God, then God's own ordered mind requires the same kind of explanation. Either an infinite regress of minds-behind-minds follows, or some mind is self-explanatory without further cause. But if a stopping point is allowed, why must it be a mind rather than matter itself?

⚡ TRY IT · WHOSE OBJECTION IS IT?
Match each move to the character who makes it.
1. "Making God's mind resemble a human mind reduces God to a superior creature, not a transcendent being."
2. "If order always requires a mind, then God's own highly ordered mind requires a cause too."
📚 PART 3 · WHAT THE DESIGN ARGUMENT CAN AND CANNOT ESTABLISH

By the end of Part IV, the discussion has not resolved, and Hume does not intend it to. What it can establish, at most, is that the order of the world provides some evidence for an intelligent cause. What it cannot establish, without much further argument, is the full set of attributes monotheism traditionally ascribes: unity (one God rather than many), infinity, perfection, moral character, or ongoing involvement. None of these follow directly from the observation that the universe was designed.

One of the most interesting features of Parts III and IV is the partial alliance between Philo and Demea: both reject Cleanthes' anthropomorphic God, though for entirely different reasons, Demea because it is theologically unworthy, Philo because it is philosophically unsupported. This alliance will break down once the Problem of Evil enters the conversation, which is where Week 7 begins, with a very different thinker's approach to the question of God.

💪 HOLD ONTO THIS

Hume does not demolish natural theology. He shows that its arguments are weaker than they appear, establish less than they claim, and leave real questions open. That is not the same as settling the question the other way.
