📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Socrates's most ambitious argument, and then there is no more philosophical work to do. What remains is to die.
Cebes has raised the sharpest challenge the Phaedo faces: even granting the soul's pre-existence and kinship with the eternal, how do we know it is truly indestructible rather than merely long-lasting? The Final Argument (Phaedo 95a–107b) is Socrates's most ambitious attempt to answer this. When Cebes finally assents, there is nothing left to argue. What follows is among the most celebrated passages in Western literature.

📚 PART 1 · WHAT ALWAYS BRINGS WHAT
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Can you think of two things that always go together, where one simply cannot show up without the other? (No wrong answer.)

Before arguing about the soul, Socrates first improves his account of causation. His "safe" account: something has a property because it participates in the relevant Form; a thing is beautiful because it participates in Beauty. His more sophisticated refinement: some things always bring a particular Form with them as a non-negotiable feature of their nature. Fire always brings heat; it cannot remain fire while also becoming cold. The number three always brings oddness; no arrangement of three objects comes out even.

Now the decisive move: what always brings life with it? The soul. Whatever body a soul enters, it enters alive; whatever body it departs, it departs dead. The soul is to life as fire is to heat. By the same logic that says fire never admits coldness, the soul never admits death. The soul is, by its essential nature, deathless (athanatos). And what is deathless, Socrates argues further, must also be imperishable (anolethron) — otherwise it could simply vanish rather than be killed, which would leave it vulnerable to destruction in another form. "Then the soul is immortal," Socrates concludes, and Cebes assents: "Necessarily" (Phaedo 107a).

This argument is stronger than the earlier three because it explains why the soul must persist, not merely that it has managed to so far. Honest readers still press two pressure points: is it really true the soul necessarily brings life the way fire necessarily brings heat, given fire's connection to heat has a chemical explanation the soul's connection to life lacks? And does "deathless" really guarantee "imperishable," or has Socrates smuggled in an extra step? These questions have been debated for two and a half millennia, a sign of the argument's depth, not a dismissal of it.

📚 PART 2 · THE DEATH SCENE

With the arguments finished, Socrates offers a myth of the afterlife, careful to name it as such: "To insist that this is exactly as I have described it would not be sensible. But that either this or something very like it is a correct description… this seems to me a proper conviction" (Phaedo 114d). The myth is not more argument; it translates the argument's conclusion into a picture vivid enough for the imagination to grasp what argument alone leaves abstract.

Then Socrates turns practical. When Crito asks how to bury him, Socrates gently corrects the question itself: the body is not Socrates; Socrates will have already departed. His last instruction to his friends is simply, "Follow the argument" (Phaedo 115c). He bathes, spends time with his family, and drinks the hemlock without hesitation when the hour comes, chiding his weeping friends to be calm. As the numbness rises through his body, he speaks his final words: "Crito, we owe a cock to Asclepius. Pay it and do not neglect it." (Phaedo 118a) Asclepius was the god of healing; a cock was the customary thank-offering for recovering from illness. Socrates's last words carry a quiet, characteristic joke: life in the body is the illness; death is the cure.

Phaedo closes the dialogue with a simple testimony to Echecrates: Socrates was, of all those present at that time, "the best, and also the most wise and the most just" (Phaedo 118a). Not an argument, but a life, offered as evidence that the arguments were worth making.
