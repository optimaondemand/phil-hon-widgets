📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What if politics isn't built on the highest good at all, but on the one thing everyone, saint and sinner alike, wants to avoid?
Plato and Aristotle built political philosophy around the summum bonum, the highest good. Hobbes rejects this entirely and grounds politics instead in the summum malum, the greatest evil: violent death. This lesson examines that shift and locates Hobbes in relation to every thinker this course has studied.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain why Hobbes denies there is a summum bonum, and why he grounds politics in the summum malum instead.
2
I will be able to explain the logic of the Hobbesian social contract and what it does and does not include.
3
I will be able to locate Hobbes's position against Plato, Aristotle, Mill, and Kant.

📚 PART 1 · HOBBES DENIES THE SUMMUM BONUM
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Do you think most people in a large, diverse society actually agree on what the highest good in life is? (No wrong answer.)

Hobbes states his break with the ancient tradition directly: "The philosophers of old did in vain... dispute about whether happiness was to be placed in riches, or bodily pleasures, or virtue, or some other thing. For they spoke without grounds, since there is no such finis ultimus (utmost aim) nor summum bonum (greatest good) as is spoken of in the books of the old moral philosophers." (Leviathan, ch. 11) Aristotle argued eudaimonia was the final end organizing all human activity; Plato argued the Form of the Good was the highest reality. Hobbes says: there is no such shared end. If politics cannot be organized around a shared vision of the good life, because people disagree too fundamentally about what that is, then a politics that tries to enforce one vision will necessarily oppress those who hold different ones.

📚 PART 2 · THE SUMMUM MALUM AND THE SOCIAL CONTRACT

If there is no highest good to build on, Hobbes builds instead on the summum malum, the greatest evil: violent, premature death. People disagree about the good life, but everyone, the miser and the philosopher alike, shares the desire to keep living. This universal aversion is the one thing Hobbes can use as a foundation that imposes no particular vision of the good on anyone. Politics, on this account, is not about helping people flourish in a shared way; it is about keeping them alive and secure, leaving the good life itself to individuals. This is the philosophical foundation of what is called liberalism in the philosophical sense: the state is neutral about the good life and exists to protect security and rights.

The Hobbesian social contract follows directly: rational individuals recognize the state of nature is unbearable and agree, each with everyone else, to surrender their unlimited natural right to a sovereign with authority to make and enforce law. The sovereign is not a party to the contract and cannot breach it. What is in the contract: the surrender of unlimited freedom for security. What is not in it: any agreement about the good life, virtue, the soul, or God, all of which are left to individuals.

📚 PART 3 · A PHILOSOPHICAL INVENTORY

Hobbes's position can be located against everything you have studied this semester. Tap each card to see how he answers, or challenges, the thinkers you already know.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL · HOBBES AGAINST THE COURSE
Against Plato
The Republic assumes an objective good, the Form of the Good, that a just city and soul are oriented toward. Hobbes denies any such good is accessible to political knowledge.
Against Aristotle
Aristotle says man is by nature a political animal. Hobbes says man is by nature a solitary individual whose first impulse is self-preservation; community is artificial, built to prevent the worst.
Against Mill
Mill says happiness is the highest good and can guide political arrangement. Hobbes would say Mill's happiness is just one more contested vision of the good, no more binding on everyone than any other.
Against Kant
Kant grounds morality in reason, available to all rational beings. Hobbes would say that without an external enforcer, even people who recognize the categorical imperative have no reliable motive to act on it. You need a sword.

Hobbes is not obviously right. But he identifies a permanent tension that has never been fully resolved: organizing political life around a shared vision of the good, versus organizing it around the minimal conditions for peaceful coexistence. Week 17 turns to Aristotle's Politics, written two thousand years before Hobbes but a direct challenge to everything this week has argued.
