📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Plato does not let Socrates win too easily.
We return to the Phaedo. After the Cyclical, Recollection, and Affinity Arguments (Week 9), the mood in the prison cell shifts. Simmias and Cebes bring objections serious enough that, for a moment, they seem to undo everything Socrates has built. Plato has Socrates notice the doubt settling over the room and gently chide them with a proverb before taking up each objection in turn.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to reconstruct Simmias's Harmony Objection and Socrates's three replies to it.
2
I will be able to reconstruct Cebes's Cloak Objection and explain why it is harder to answer.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Plato, Phaedo 85b-90d, assigned excerpt: “Two Objections to the Soul”
📚 PART 1 · SIMMIAS'S HARMONY OBJECTION
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of a beautiful piece of music. Does the beauty of the performance feel like it exists independently of the instrument, or entirely dependent on it? (No wrong answer.)

Simmias says everything Socrates has established about the soul is also true of a harmony, the tuning of a lyre. The lyre's harmony is invisible, intangible, and excellent, exactly the qualities Socrates admires. But the harmony depends entirely on the strings being properly tensed. Destroy the lyre, and the harmony ceases; there is no harmony floating above the wreckage. Perhaps the soul is exactly this: the harmony of the body's physical elements, real, invisible, wonderful, and entirely dependent on the body for its existence. This is a sophisticated ancient version of a view many contemporary philosophers still hold.

Socrates's replies (91c-95a): First, a harmony cannot oppose the elements that constitute it, but the soul regularly opposes the body, resisting hunger, mastering fear. A harmony that could overrule its own strings would be a contradiction. Second, the Recollection Argument already established the soul existed before the body, but a harmony cannot precede the instrument it harmonizes. Third, if the soul were a harmony, all souls would be equally souls, since every harmony is equally a harmony, and no soul could be more virtuous than another. But virtue and vice clearly admit of degree.

⚡ TRY IT · WHICH REPLY IS THIS?
Match each reply to what it shows.
1. "The soul resists hunger and masters fear, but a harmony can never overrule the very strings that produce it."
2. "The Recollection Argument showed the soul knew the Forms before this body existed; a harmony cannot precede its instrument."
📚 PART 2 · CEBES'S CLOAK OBJECTION

Cebes's objection is subtler, and many readers think harder to answer. He grants nearly everything: suppose the soul does pre-exist the body, survives individual deaths, and moves from body to body. This still does not establish immortality in the full, indestructible sense; it only establishes that the soul outlasts any particular body. A weaver makes many cloaks in a lifetime, each outlasting many seasons of wear, but the weaver himself eventually dies too, and each cloak eventually wears out. If someone argued the weaver must still be alive because his cloak is still in good condition, they would be reasoning poorly.

Applied to the soul: even if it survives death and enters new bodies across many lifetimes, it may be gradually wearing down with each passage, until it finally gives out. The Cyclical and Recollection Arguments show the soul persists through individual deaths; they do not show it is imperishable. Socrates agrees this objection requires a better answer than he has given, and acknowledges a real danger: if students learn to follow careful arguments and then watch them refuted, they may become misologists, haters of argument, concluding no philosophical reasoning can be trusted. This danger, Socrates says, is worse than the danger of dying (Phaedo 89d–90d). The right response to a failed argument is not despair but a better argument, which is exactly what Lesson 11b provides.

💪 HOLD ONTO THIS

Cebes accepts almost everything Socrates has argued and still denies the conclusion. That is what a genuinely strong objection looks like: not a rejection of the premises, but a demonstration that they do not yet reach the conclusion.
