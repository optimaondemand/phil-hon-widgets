How to Test a Definition: Euthyphro's Three Attempts
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Euthyphro offers three definitions of piety. Watching Socrates take each one apart teaches you how to test any definition.
Euthyphro is standing on the steps of the King Archon's court, about to prosecute his own father, and he is doing it in the name of piety. If he is going to appeal to piety as his justification, Socrates wants to understand what piety actually is. What follows is one of Plato's clearest lessons in philosophical method, and this lesson gives you a precise tool, necessary and sufficient conditions, for understanding exactly why each of Euthyphro's answers fails.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to define necessary and sufficient conditions and use them to evaluate a definition.
2
I will be able to explain why each of Euthyphro's first two definitions of piety fails, in terms of necessity and sufficiency.
3
I will be able to state the three general tests a definition must pass.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Plato's Euthyphro, assigned excerpt: “The Euthyphro Dilemma”
📚 PART 1 · WHAT SOCRATES IS LOOKING FOR

Before examining the definitions, we need to understand what kind of answer Socrates wants. He is not asking for examples of piety. He wants its essence, the one thing that makes all pious acts pious: “Tell me what is the nature of this idea, and then I shall have a standard to which I may look, and by which I may measure actions… and then I shall be able to say that such and such an action is pious, such another impious.” (Euthyphro, 6e)

A list of pious acts will not give him a standard, for the same reason that pointing to gold coins, gold rings, and gold teeth does not tell you what gold is. A definition should give us the principle that explains why each example qualifies.

A TOOL FOR TESTING: NECESSARY AND SUFFICIENT CONDITIONS
A condition is necessary for piety if nothing can be pious without it. If you can find a pious act that lacks the condition, the condition is not necessary.
A condition is sufficient for piety if having it guarantees the act is pious. If you can find something that has the condition but is not pious, or the condition produces a contradiction, the condition is not sufficient.
A good definition of piety needs a condition that is both: present in every pious act, and enough by itself to guarantee an act is pious. Watch for which way each of Euthyphro's answers fails.
📚 PART 2 · DEFINITION ONE: PIETY IS WHAT I AM DOING
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Before you read Socrates' response: do you think "prosecuting wrongdoers, even your own family" is a good definition of piety? (No wrong answer.)

Euthyphro's first response points to an example: “Piety is doing as I am doing; that is to say, prosecuting any one who is guilty of murder, sacrilege, or of any similar crime, whether he be your father or mother, or whoever he may be, that makes no difference.” (Euthyphro, 5d–e)

Socrates grants that Euthyphro's action may indeed be pious. But even so, citing it does not answer the question. There are many pious acts, and Euthyphro has pointed to one of them. What Socrates asked for is the general idea that makes all of them pious. Think of the counterexample: is a quiet act of temple sacrifice pious? Almost certainly yes, and it involves no prosecution of anyone. So prosecuting wrongdoers cannot be a necessary condition for piety: there are pious acts that do not have it. Euthyphro has the order backward. He is saying, in effect, “piety is like what I'm doing right now,” which tells us nothing about cases that look very different from his.

📚 PART 3 · DEFINITION TWO: PIETY IS WHAT THE GODS LOVE

Euthyphro tries again with something genuinely general: “Piety, then, is that which is dear to the gods, and impiety is that which is not dear to them.” (Euthyphro, 7a) This is a real improvement: rather than pointing to a particular act, Euthyphro offers a criterion that could apply to any action.

But Socrates identifies a problem using something Euthyphro has already conceded: the gods quarrel, and what they quarrel about is precisely questions of justice and right, the same questions at issue in Euthyphro's case. If different gods love and hate the same things, then on Euthyphro's own definition, one and the same act would be both pious and impious at once: “Then the same things are hated by the gods and loved by the gods, and are both hateful and dear to them? And upon this view the same things, Euthyphro, will be pious and also impious?” (Euthyphro, 8a)

Being loved by a god turns out not to be a sufficient condition for piety: it does not guarantee the act is pious, because the very same act can also be hated by a different god. A definition that makes the same act both pious and impious is not a definition at all. It has failed to distinguish the very things it was supposed to define.

📚 PART 4 · DEFINITION THREE, IN BRIEF

Socrates proposes a revision: what all the gods love is pious, and what all of them hate is impious. Euthyphro accepts this, and the contradiction from Definition Two dissolves, since unanimous approval leaves no room for the same act being both loved and hated. This time, the condition may genuinely be both necessary and sufficient: perhaps it really is true that an act is pious if and only if all the gods love it.

But Socrates is still not satisfied, and he raises the dialogue's deepest question: is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or is it pious because it is loved by the gods? Even a condition that gets the necessary and sufficient facts right can still fail to tell us what piety is, as opposed to something that merely always accompanies it. This question has come to be called the Euthyphro Dilemma, and it deserves its own full treatment: Lesson 2c takes it up in detail.

📚 PART 5 · THE METHOD

By now the pattern is clear. Each time Euthyphro offers a definition, Socrates takes it seriously and asks what follows from it. Three tests emerge, and they can be applied to any definition: does it give a principle rather than an example (is the condition even a general one)? Does it avoid contradiction (is the condition sufficient)? Does it reach the essence rather than an accompanying attribute?

⚡ TRY IT · NECESSARY OR INSUFFICIENT?
For each of Euthyphro's first two definitions, identify exactly how it fails.
1. Definition One: piety is prosecuting wrongdoers, regardless of relation
2. Definition Two: piety is what the gods love

Notice that Socrates does not need to know the correct answer to apply these tests. He takes the proposed definition, follows it where it leads, and lets it stand or fall on its own logic. The dialogue ends without a definition of piety that survives. That, for Plato, is the beginning of philosophy.
