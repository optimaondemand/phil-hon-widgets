Hume's Method: How to Argue About God
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A subject too sublime and uncertain for one voice to settle alone.
David Hume was one of the most celebrated and most feared philosophers in Europe: celebrated for his clarity and wit, feared for his willingness to press skeptical arguments wherever they led. He wrote his Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion over roughly twenty-five years and arranged for it to appear only after his death in 1779. Before reading his arguments, you need to understand why he chose the dialogue form, and who is speaking.

📚 PART 1 · WHY WRITE A DIALOGUE?
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
On a question this contested, would you trust a single confident author more, or a conversation where several smart people disagree openly? (No wrong answer.)

Hume chose the dialogue form because he believed no single character could honestly claim to have the complete argument on a question "so sublime and uncertain." A dialogue allows a writer to present arguments on all sides with full force, without forcing a premature resolution. This creates a genuine puzzle for readers: which character speaks for Hume? He provides no obvious answer. Most careful readers conclude that Philo voices Hume's own views most directly, but Hume uses the form to give Cleanthes' position genuine force too, because genuine force is what honest philosophy requires.

📚 PART 2 · THE THREE VOICES

Tap each name to see who they are and what they argue.

Cleanthes
The empirical theist. Argues God's existence can be established from the evidence of design in nature, by analogy with human artifacts. Confident, careful, willing to follow the argument wherever it leads.
Philo
The philosophical skeptic. Not necessarily an atheist; relentlessly doubtful about whether human reason can establish anything about a being so far removed from ordinary experience. Presses analogies until they break.
Demea
The orthodox rationalist. Believes in God through a priori reasoning, not empirical observation, and prefers a God whose nature is incomprehensible to human reason. Allies with Philo at points, for opposite reasons.
📚 PART 3 · THE OPENING, AND PHILO'S FIRST WARNING

The dialogue opens with a question that is not merely a warm-up: at what age is it appropriate to begin religious instruction? Demea argues for early childhood, before doubt can take root. Philo agrees, with characteristic irony, but for the reverse reason: he believes religion cannot be established by reason at all, so it might as well be instilled by custom first. This exchange introduces the dialogue's central tension: whether religious belief and reason belong together or apart.

Philo's broader warning in Part I is that human reason was built to navigate the human world: causal inferences about familiar objects, practical decisions, ordinary experience. Applying it to questions about what exists beyond the visible universe stretches a tool far outside the domain it was built for. Cleanthes pushes back: abandon reason on religious questions entirely, and you cannot defend religion against its critics either. Better to engage reason carefully and see what it actually establishes.

📚 PART 4 · PART II: THE DESIGN ARGUMENT STATED

In Part II, Cleanthes offers what he considers the most compelling argument for God's existence. "The curious adapting of means to ends, throughout all nature, resembles exactly, though it much exceeds, the productions of human contrivance… we are led to infer, by all the rules of analogy, that the causes also resemble; and that the Author of Nature is somewhat similar to the mind of man, though possessed of much larger faculties." This is the argument from analogy in its clearest form: nature resembles human artifacts; human artifacts are produced by intelligent design; therefore nature is produced by something resembling an intelligent designer, only far greater.

Philo immediately begins his critique, and it will occupy the rest of this week's reading. Lesson 6b follows Philo's arguments through Parts II and III, examining exactly where he believes the analogy breaks down.
