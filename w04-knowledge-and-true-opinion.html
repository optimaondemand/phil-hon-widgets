Lesson 4b
Knowledge and True Opinion: A Crucial Distinction
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Two guides can both get you to Larissa. Only one of them actually knows the road.
The Meno has shown us that knowledge is within us, recoverable through inquiry, and that it belongs to the soul rather than to any social category. But the original question, can virtue be taught, still needs an answer. To approach it properly, Socrates introduces a distinction that is one of the most important he ever makes: the distinction between knowledge and true opinion. It sounds simple. It is not.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to distinguish knowledge from true opinion using the road-to-Larissa example.
2
I will be able to explain why Socrates says true opinions must be "tethered" to be reliable.
3
I will be able to explain why the failure of Athenian statesmen to teach virtue to their own sons is evidence against virtue being ordinary knowledge.

📚 PART 1 · THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN KNOWING AND BELIEVING CORRECTLY
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of something you can do correctly but couldn't fully explain to someone else. How common do you think that is? (No wrong answer.)

Suppose you need to travel from Athens to Larissa, and you ask two people for directions. The first has never been to Larissa but heard someone describe the road and remembered it correctly. The second has made the journey many times and knows the road from experience. Both give you the right directions. Both would lead you to Larissa. But there is a difference between them.

Socrates puts it this way: “If a man knew the way to Larissa, or anywhere else, and went and led others there, he would be a right and good guide would he not? And a person who had a right opinion about the way, but had never gone and did not know, might he not be an equally good guide?” (Meno, 97a–b) For the purpose of arriving in Larissa, both guides work equally well. But for understanding why the road goes where it does, or explaining it to others, or recognizing it under different conditions, only the person with knowledge is equipped. The difference is not in the content of what is believed; both believe the same true thing. The difference is in how the belief is held and what grounds it.

📚 PART 2 · WHY TRUE OPINION IS UNSTABLE

Socrates uses an image to explain why true opinion, however useful, is not the same as knowledge. He says true opinions are like the legendary statues of Daedalus, beautiful and lifelike, but prone to running away if not fastened down: “True opinions are a fine thing and do all sorts of good so long as they stay in their place, but they will not stay long. They run away from a man's mind; so they are not worth much until you tether them by working out the reason why.” (Meno, 97e–98a)

An opinion with no account behind it, no explanation of why it is true, is unstable. It may be correct today and gone tomorrow. It may serve you in familiar circumstances and fail you when the situation changes. Knowledge is opinion with an account, meta logou, as Plato says: true belief tied down, reasoned through, that understands why it is true and can therefore assess cases the original inquiry never directly considered.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
Statues of Daedalus
Legendary statues said to be so lifelike they could move and had to be tied down or they would wander off. Socrates' image for true opinion: beautiful and useful, but unreliable until "tethered" by an account of why it is true.
📚 PART 3 · WHAT CAN AND CANNOT BE TAUGHT

This distinction bears directly on the question of virtue. If virtue were a form of knowledge, a systematic understanding of what is good and how to pursue it, it could in principle be taught: knowledge is communicable, and a teacher who genuinely knows a subject can transmit that understanding through instruction. But the Meno runs into a problem here. If virtue is teachable knowledge, there must be teachers of it, and who are they?

The Sophists claim to teach virtue and charge large fees for it, but many prominent Athenians did not believe they actually did. And there is a telling piece of evidence: great and virtuous Athenian statesmen, Themistocles, Aristides, Pericles, consistently failed to produce virtuous sons. Pericles, whose excellence was widely acknowledged, had sons widely regarded as undistinguished. “And yet,” Socrates notes, “if virtue could be taught by anyone, it would have been by him.” (Meno, 94b) If the most virtuous men in Athens could not reliably transmit their virtue to their own children, under the most favorable possible conditions, virtue does not appear to be the kind of thing taught and learned the way geometry is.

📚 PART 4 · ACTING WELL WITHOUT KNOWING WHY

Socrates proposes a different explanation for why some people act virtuously: not knowledge, not instruction, but something closer to true opinion combined with a kind of guidance from outside the person. “So those who guide states aright, statesmen and poets, are not wiser than other men, but are inspired, though they know not what they say.” (Meno, 99d) The person who acts well because of true opinion, without knowledge, is genuinely acting well; the outcomes may be as good as those of the person who acts from knowledge. But they cannot reliably repeat their success under new conditions, cannot explain what they did, and cannot identify when they are being led astray by a plausible but false belief.
