What Is Utilitarianism?

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
"It is better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied." What could that possibly mean, coming from a philosopher who thinks pleasure is the whole of the good?
In 1863, John Stuart Mill published Utilitarianism, an eighty-page defense of the view that an action is right if it produces more happiness than any alternative, and wrong if it produces less. This is the Principle of Utility, and it answers Glaucon's challenge in a completely different key than Plato did: not through the soul's inner structure, but through the felt experience of happiness itself.

📚 PART 1 · WHO WAS MILL, AND WHAT DOES HE CLAIM?
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
If you had to name the one thing everyone ultimately wants, for its own sake and nothing beyond it, what would you say? (No wrong answer.)

John Stuart Mill (1806–1873) was raised by his father James Mill, a close associate of the utilitarian founder Jeremy Bentham, on an extraordinary regime of home education: Greek at age three, logic and political economy as a teenager. In his mid-twenties he suffered what he called a "mental crisis," a period in which the utilitarian worldview he had been raised to accept suddenly felt hollow. He recovered partly through poetry and partly by rethinking utilitarianism to give far more weight to the quality of pleasures, not merely their quantity. That rethinking runs through Utilitarianism.

Mill's foundational claim: happiness is the only thing desirable as an end in itself. Everything else people value, money, power, fame, knowledge, is valued because it produces happiness or has become associated with it. If happiness is the ultimate end, then the right action in any situation is whichever one best promotes it. "The creed which accepts as the foundation of morals ‘utility’ or the ‘greatest happiness principle’ holds that actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness." (Utilitarianism, Ch. II)

📚 PART 2 · HIGHER AND LOWER PLEASURES

If happiness just means pleasure and the absence of pain, the theory seems to reduce morality to calculating pleasures like a grocer counting inventory. Bentham had embraced this implication directly, proposing a "hedonic calculus" across seven dimensions of pleasure. Critics mocked the whole approach as "a doctrine worthy only of swine": if happiness is just pleasure, a pig rolling in mud is doing as well, philosophically, as a person reading Plato.

Mill's reply is the distinction between higher and lower pleasures. Some pleasures, intellectual activity, artistic appreciation, friendship, moral development, are qualitatively superior to others, not merely more intense or longer-lasting but of a different and higher kind. How do we know which are higher? Mill's test is empirical: ask those who have experienced both. People who have experienced intellectual and purely sensory pleasures consistently prefer the intellectual, and Mill takes that preference as a genuine recognition that one kind of life is deeper and better. "It is better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied. And if the fool, or the pig, are of a different opinion, it is because they only know their own side of the question." (Utilitarianism, Ch. II)

📚 PART 3 · MILL'S ANSWER TO GLAUCON

Mill's theory is, in its own way, a direct response to the question Glaucon raised in the Republic. Glaucon asked whether justice is genuinely good for the person who has it. Mill's answer: the question is really about happiness. The just life, one of integrity, fairness, and genuine regard for others, is better because it produces more happiness, for the person living it and for those around them.

But notice how differently Mill gets there than Plato does. Plato located the good life in the proper structure of the soul, reason governing appetite, each part fulfilling its function. Mill locates it in the experience of pleasure and the absence of pain. Plato's account is metaphysical and looks inward at the soul's constitution; Mill's is empirical and looks outward at the felt quality of experience. This is a genuinely different strategy for answering the same question, and it faces a different kind of pressure: Plato can explain why the tyrant is miserable even with everything he wants, because his soul is disordered. Mill has to explain the same conclusion differently, by arguing that the tyrant's pleasures are lower-grade, or that the suffering he causes to others must count against his own happiness calculus.

💪 HOLD ONTO THIS

Mill wants a theory grounded entirely in pleasure, yet insists some pleasures matter more than others in kind, not just amount. Holding both of those claims together is the whole difficulty, and the whole interest, of his ethics.
