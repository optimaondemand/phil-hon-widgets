📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Small letters at a distance are hard to read. Socrates proposes reading them large first, in a whole city, before turning back to the single soul.
Before Socrates can answer Glaucon's challenge, he has to solve a prior problem: nobody in the dialogue has yet given a satisfying definition of justice. This lesson traces Plato's reframing of the question, from "what behavior counts as just" to "what is a just soul," and gives Plato's first answer to Glaucon: that justice is a certain kind of internal harmony.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain why Socrates examines justice in the city before turning to the soul.
2
I will be able to name and describe the three parts of the soul in Plato's account: reason, spirit, and appetite.
3
I will be able to state Plato's definition of justice as harmony of the soul, and explain how it answers Glaucon.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Plato, Republic II (368c–end), selections from Book IV on the tripartite soul
📚 PART 1 · THE PROBLEM OF SCALE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
If someone asked you right now to define justice in one sentence, how confident would you feel? (No wrong answer.)

Socrates proposes a method: justice in a single soul may be like small letters written at a distance, hard to read clearly. If the same letters were written large, in a whole city, they might be easier to make out, and that larger picture could then be brought back to the individual. This is why the Republic spends so much time building an imagined city with three classes, producers, soldiers, and philosopher-rulers, each doing the work it is suited for, none overreaching into another's domain. Plato is not primarily giving political advice; the city is a philosophical instrument for magnifying the structure of the soul.

📚 PART 2 · THE TRIPARTITE SOUL

The city's three classes map onto three parts of the individual soul. Tap each card below to see how Plato describes it.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL · THE THREE PARTS OF THE SOUL
Reason
logos
The calculating, truth-seeking part. It thinks things through, weighs evidence, and asks what is genuinely good rather than merely pleasant. Its proper role is to govern the other two parts.
Spirit
thumos
The energetic, honor-seeking, indignant part. It flares up at insults and injustice, including its own. Properly trained, it becomes reason's ally, defending what reason identifies as good.
Appetite
epithumia
The desiring part: food, drink, comfort, money, physical pleasure. Not bad in itself, but the largest, most powerful, and least rational part of the soul.

A just soul is one in which reason governs, spirit supports reason, and appetite is directed by their guidance. Each part performs its proper function; reason rules not by suppressing appetite and spirit but by ordering them well. An unjust soul reverses this order: if appetite rules, reason becomes a servant devising clever schemes for satisfying desire rather than evaluating it; if spirit is misdirected toward domination rather than genuine goods, the soul is unstable and at war with itself. This is why Socrates insists the tyrant, who satisfies every appetite and answers to no one, is in fact the most miserable person in the city: his soul is the most disordered, his reason enslaved to his lowest impulses.

📚 PART 3 · ANSWERING GLAUCON, AND WHAT REMAINS OPEN

Glaucon asked whether justice is good for the person who has it, even stripped of every social reward. Plato's answer is yes, and the reason is what it does to the soul. Someone who systematically subordinates reason to appetite or to the lust for power has disordered the very part of themselves that is best and most truly them. Whatever external success they enjoy, they have damaged the inner structure that makes genuine flourishing possible. Justice, on this account, is not a constraint imposed from outside; it is the soul's own internal health.

This answer is powerful, but it raises its own questions, and those questions drive the rest of Module 4. It depends on a specific theory of the soul: if the soul does not actually have three parts standing in this hierarchy, the account weakens. It also focuses heavily on the agent's own inner state; the Crito raised justice as a question about what we owe to others, and it is not obvious that internal harmony alone answers that. And Plato assumes reason ought to be the ruling part: Mill will argue instead for happiness as the criterion, Kant for duty and rational dignity, and Aristotle for an integration of reason and desire rather than the rule of one over the other. Each of these thinkers is still answering Glaucon. The conversation does not end with the Republic; it begins there.
