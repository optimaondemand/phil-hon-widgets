📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The Meno ends with an answer its own author will spend the rest of his life revising.
Socrates' final answer about virtue, that it comes to those who have it as a kind of divine gift, is offered explicitly as a provisional conclusion, uncertain and in need of further investigation. To read it as Plato's last word is to misread it. To read it as a genuine, considered position that reveals the limits of the earlier argument is to read it correctly. This closing lesson of Module 1 examines that conclusion, and the questions it deliberately leaves open for the rest of the course.

📚 PART 1 · THE ARGUMENT FROM THE ABSENCE OF TEACHERS
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Do you think moral goodness is something a person achieves through their own effort, or something more like a gift they happen to receive? (No wrong answer; philosophers disagree about this too.)

The final movement of the Meno runs as follows. Virtue, if it is knowledge, should be teachable. If it is teachable, there should be teachers of it. The Sophists claim to be teachers of virtue, but their results are disputed. The genuinely virtuous statesmen of Athens consistently failed to produce virtuous sons, even when they clearly tried. This suggests that whatever virtue is, it is not transmitted through instruction the way mathematics is.

If virtue cannot be taught, and the evidence suggests it cannot, then it is not a form of knowledge in the full sense. Socrates proposes that virtuous men succeed by a kind of correct opinion that comes to them by divine favor, the way poets are said to be inspired by the Muses. “Then virtue is neither natural nor acquired by teaching, but comes by divine dispensation, without reason, to those whom it comes to. Unless some statesman should arise who is capable of making another into a statesman.” (Meno, 99e–100a)

📚 PART 2 · READING THE CONCLUSION CAREFULLY

There is a crucial qualification in that passage: unless some statesman should arise who is capable of making another into a statesman. This exception is not incidental. It is the door the Republic and the rest of the Platonic corpus will walk through. What would make someone capable of genuinely transmitting virtue rather than merely having correct opinions about it? Socrates' answer, developed elsewhere: knowledge. Not true opinion, but genuine understanding of what virtue is and why it is good.

The Meno's conclusion, then, is not that virtue is permanently and essentially unteachable. It is that virtue is unteachable by people who lack knowledge of what virtue is, and since the Meno itself never successfully completed a definition of virtue, of course we cannot teach it: you cannot teach what you do not understand. This gives the dialogue a kind of built-in self-correction. The next question is: what would it take to actually know what virtue is? That question is the agenda of the next decade of Platonic philosophy, and of this course.

📚 PART 3 · TAKING THE DIVINE GIFT SERIOUSLY

The claim that virtuous people receive their virtue as a gift is not merely rhetorical. The virtuous conduct of the great Athenian statesmen was not, so far as one could tell, the result of their own explicit reasoning. Themistocles did not write a treatise on strategy and derive his decisions from first principles. They acted well, and the results were genuinely good, but the capacity seems to have been, in some important sense, given rather than achieved. There is a long tradition, in Plato, in Augustine, in Aquinas, of treating the virtues of outstanding individuals as graced rather than self-generated: the idea is not that human effort is irrelevant, but that the capacity for genuine moral excellence is not fully within the reach of unaided human will.

For students who approach this from a religious perspective, the claim will resonate; the idea that genuine goodness is partly a gift rather than an achievement is a recognizable note in Christian moral theology. For students without that frame, the philosophical point still stands: the evidence from the statesmen's lives suggests their excellence was not the product of a method they understood and could pass on. Its source was, in the relevant sense, opaque to them.

📚 PART 4 · WHAT THE MENO LEAVES OPEN

The Meno is explicitly incomplete. Socrates says as much himself: “We cannot know for certain about this until we have first inquired into what virtue itself is.” (Meno, 100b, paraphrased) Several things remain genuinely open, and this course will take each of them up in turn.

Is virtue teachable? The Meno says no, given current circumstances, but allows it could become teachable if someone with genuine knowledge existed. The Republic explores what that person would look like.
What is the relationship between virtue and knowledge? Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics, later in this course, will argue that virtue is not a matter of knowing a principle at all, but of having a well-trained character.
What is the soul, and how does it relate to virtue? The Phaedo, later this year, takes up this question directly.
What is the good, and can we know it? The Republic argues that all genuine knowledge ultimately rests on knowledge of the Form of the Good, the highest and most difficult object of philosophical inquiry.
📚 PART 5 · THE INVITATION COMPLETED

Module 1 is called “An Invitation to Philosophy.” The invitation has now been fully issued. The Apology showed us what a philosophical life looks like, at its most demanding, in the face of death, and told us the unexamined life is not worth living. The Euthyphro showed us how philosophical examination works: what kind of questions it asks, what a definition needs to accomplish, what productive puzzlement looks like. The Meno showed us what inquiry is for and what it presupposes: a theory of the soul's capacity for knowledge, a distinction between genuine understanding and merely having the right answer, and an honest acknowledgment that the most important questions are still open.

These three dialogues do not give us the answers. They give us something more useful: a clear picture of what the answers would need to be like, what it would take to have them, and why the inquiry is worth undertaking. The next units take up the great questions, about God, about the soul, about the good life and justice and community, with the methods and the honest humility the Apology, the Euthyphro, and the Meno have put in your hands.
