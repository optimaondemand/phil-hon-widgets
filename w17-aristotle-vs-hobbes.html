📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A city executed its best philosopher for asking too many questions. Hobbes says the sovereign was within its rights. Aristotle says the city betrayed its own purpose. Who is right?
Hobbes says political community is artificial, built to prevent the worst. Aristotle says it is natural, built to achieve the best. This closing lesson on political philosophy draws that contrast to its sharpest point, tests each thinker's weakest point, and asks what the disagreement means for Socrates's trial and death, the event this whole course began with.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to state the six-question comparison between Hobbes and Aristotle on human nature, the state of nature, and the purpose of political community.
2
I will be able to evaluate each philosopher's strongest and weakest points against the other.
3
I will be able to explain how Hobbes and Aristotle would each assess Socrates's trial and execution.

📚 PART 1 · SIX QUESTIONS, TWO ANSWERS
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Having read both, do you find yourself leaning more toward Hobbes or more toward Aristotle on what political community is for? (No wrong answer.)

The root disagreement is about human nature. Hobbes: human beings are naturally isolated individuals, motivated by self-preservation; political community is fear's remedy for the state of nature. Aristotle: human beings are naturally political; the polis is where human nature reaches its telos, and the impulse toward community is nature completing itself, not escaping itself. Every other disagreement, about the state of nature, the foundation of authority, the definition of justice, the highest human activity, flows from this root.

📚 PART 2 · TESTING EACH THEORY'S WEAKEST POINT

The standard objection to Hobbes: his state of nature is unrealistic, since human beings have never existed as pre-social isolated individuals; there is no pre-social human being, because the anthropological record shows people have always lived in groups. Hobbes anticipated this: the state of nature is a structural claim about the logic of interaction among self-interested agents, not a historical description. Aristotle's sharper reply: Hobbes's "isolated individual" is already a social product, since the language, reason, and even the self-preservation instinct Hobbes attributes to natural man are all developed through social life. The Hobbesian starting point may smuggle in social products while claiming to start from a pre-social baseline.

The standard objection to Aristotle: his teleological framework, the claim that nature has purposes, is deeply unfashionable in modern science, which favors efficient causes over final causes. Why think political community is the purpose of human nature, rather than one arrangement humans happen to have hit upon? Aristotle's defender need not posit a cosmic designer; noticing that human beings have characteristic capacities, reason, speech, deliberation about the good, that are most fully exercised in some social contexts and not others, is enough to call the polis their natural home.

⚡ WEIGH IT · WHICH OBJECTION LANDS HARDER?
Considering both objections above, which theory's weak point do you find more damaging: Hobbes's unrealistic state of nature, or Aristotle's unfashionable teleology?
📚 PART 3 · WHERE THIS COURSE BEGAN

Think back to the Apology, where Socrates was tried and executed for corrupting the youth and impiety. That trial tested the relationship between the individual and the political community: does the city have legitimate authority over the individual's pursuit of truth and virtue? Socrates died insisting the examined life mattered more than political compliance.

Hobbes would say: the sovereign's authority is unconditional once the social contract is made; Socrates should have obeyed. Aristotle would say: a city that executes its best philosopher is a defective city, one that has betrayed the very purpose, the good life, that political community exists to serve. Neither position is obviously correct, but understanding the philosophical roots of this disagreement, tracing it back to a fundamental question about human nature and the purpose of political community, helps you engage every version of this debate, in every generation, more seriously and honestly.
