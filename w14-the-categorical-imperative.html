📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Could everyone act on your reason for lying, and the practice of promising still survive?
Kant has established that the supreme principle of morality must be a categorical imperative, unconditional, universal, binding regardless of desire. This lesson works through his two major formulations of that principle, the Formula of Universal Law and the Formula of Humanity, and applies both to real cases.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain the Formula of Universal Law and apply the universalizability test to a proposed maxim.
2
I will be able to explain the Formula of Humanity and what it means to treat someone "merely as a means."
3
I will be able to explain the Kingdom of Ends and how it draws the two formulas into a single picture of morality.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Immanuel Kant, Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, Section II (selections)
📚 PART 1 · THE FORMULA OF UNIVERSAL LAW
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of the last small rule you bent for yourself ("just this once"). If everyone bent that exact rule whenever it was convenient, would the world still work the same way? (No wrong answer.)

The first formulation: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law." (Groundwork, 4:421) A maxim is the principle underlying an action. The test: take your proposed maxim, imagine everyone following it universally, and ask whether that world is even coherent, and whether you could rationally will it. Two failures are possible: a logical contradiction (the universalized maxim undermines itself, as with false promising, which destroys the very practice of trust it depends on), or a practical contradiction (the maxim is conceivable but cannot be rationally willed, as with refusing to ever help others, since any rational agent will eventually need help themselves).

📚 PART 2 · THE FORMULA OF HUMANITY

The second formulation: "Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in that of another, always as an end and never as a means only." (Groundwork, 4:429) Every rational being has dignity, a worth incomparable to and unexchangeable for anything else. The formula does not forbid using people as means at all, we do that constantly, hiring a plumber, riding a bus; it forbids treating them merely as means, bypassing their rational agency, their capacity to consent and to be reasoned with, as if they were tools rather than persons.

⚡ TRY IT · WHICH TEST DOES THIS FAIL?
For each case, identify which formulation of the categorical imperative it most directly violates.
1. Borrowing money with a promise to repay it, while secretly intending never to repay
2. Exploiting a friend's insecurities to manipulate them into agreeing with your plan
📚 PART 3 · THE KINGDOM OF ENDS

The two formulas approach morality from different angles but converge in verdict almost every time. Universal Law asks whether your principle could be shared by all rational beings; Humanity asks whether your action respects every person's status as a rational agent. Together they suggest morality is essentially interpersonal: what makes an action wrong is not merely its bad consequences but its failure to treat persons with the respect rational agency demands.

Kant draws both formulas into a single image: the Kingdom of Ends, a possible community in which every member is treated as both legislator and subject of the moral law, governed by principles all could collectively endorse as rational agents. On the common objection that this makes morality cold, that helping someone from love rather than duty seems to have no moral worth on Kant's view, his answer is careful: love and duty are not rivals. Duty adds reliability precisely where feeling might fade; when you are tired, resentful, or the affection has cooled, duty remains as a motivation. Both matter, but they answer different questions.
