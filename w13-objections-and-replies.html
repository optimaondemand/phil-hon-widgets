📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
In 1729, a satirist proposed, in the calm tone of an economic policy paper, that the poor sell their children as food. He did not mean it. That is exactly the point.
Any moral theory powerful enough to matter will face serious objections. Mill knew this, and in Chapter II of Utilitarianism he takes on the strongest attacks against his own view. This lesson works through four major objections and Mill's replies, then turns to a satirical text written more than a century before Mill, that dramatizes exactly what happens when aggregate calculation is allowed to operate without any independent limit.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to state the four major objections to utilitarianism covered in this lesson and evaluate Mill's replies to each.
2
I will be able to explain how Jonathan Swift's A Modest Proposal satirizes purely aggregative moral reasoning, and why it is satire rather than a sincere argument.
3
I will be able to explain what utilitarianism establishes despite these objections, and why the problem of persons points toward Kant's alternative.

📚 PART 1 · FOUR OBJECTIONS
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Of the objections you've encountered so far to utilitarianism, which worries you most: that it demands too much calculation, that it can justify injustice, or that it flattens special relationships into the same calculus as strangers? (No wrong answer.)

Objection One: a doctrine worthy only of swine. If the good is pleasure, morality seems to sink to the level of animal gratification. Mill's reply is the higher/lower pleasures distinction: human pleasures, engaging higher faculties, are categorically superior, not merely different. The unresolved question: why should pleasure be the criterion at all, even qualitatively enriched pleasure? Kant will argue that some things matter independent of whether they produce pleasure at all.

Objection Two: it is too demanding to calculate. Mill's reply is that accumulated human experience has already encoded utilitarian wisdom into our ordinary moral rules, honesty, promise-keeping, avoiding harm, so we do not need to recalculate from scratch. This is the move toward rule utilitarianism, and critics note the tension: if utility ultimately matters, why follow a rule when breaking it seems to produce more good?

Objection Three: it is compatible with injustice. The sheriff case is the classic example: sacrificing one innocent person for the sake of five looks, by the numbers, like the right call, but intuition strongly resists it. Mill argues that security from arbitrary punishment is such an enormous component of happiness that violating it almost always produces more disutility than it prevents, and in Chapter V he treats justice as a specially weighted category of utility. What he cannot fully explain, on strictly utilitarian grounds, is why individual rights should function as constraints rather than merely as one factor among others. That gap points toward Kant's territory.

📚 PART 2 · A SATIRICAL ILLUSTRATION: SWIFT'S A MODEST PROPOSAL

In 1729, more than a century before Mill wrote Utilitarianism, the Anglo-Irish satirist Jonathan Swift published a pamphlet titled A Modest Proposal. Writing in the calm, reasonable voice of an economic reformer, Swift's narrator proposes that impoverished Irish families sell their one-year-old children as food for wealthy households, thereby relieving poverty, providing income for the poor, and reducing a "surplus population," all argued through careful cost-benefit reasoning, complete with projected yields and prices.

Swift's "proposal" is satire, not a sincere recommendation. He is impersonating the voice of pure aggregative calculation to expose what happens when a way of reasoning tracks only the arithmetic of costs and benefits, without any independent principle protecting persons from being treated as commodities. The narrator's tone is perfectly reasonable and procedurally rigorous throughout; that composure is precisely the horror. He never once asks whether children have a claim not to be eaten regardless of how the numbers work out.

Swift is not attacking Mill specifically; Utilitarianism would not exist for over a hundred years. But the pamphlet dramatizes exactly the danger named in Objection Three: a moral calculus that tracks only aggregate welfare, with no independent constraint of justice, can in principle be made to justify atrocity, provided the numbers are arranged correctly. This is why the essay belongs here rather than as a stand-alone literary curiosity: it gives the abstract worry about aggregation a face, and a queasy one.

📚 PART 3 · WHAT UTILITARIANISM ESTABLISHES, AND THE TURN TO KANT

Objection Four: it ignores the special claims of relationships. Utilitarianism requires impartiality, but our moral lives are not impartial; we owe our children and friends something that does not reduce to their contribution to aggregate welfare. Mill's theory can only say that strong relationships are instrumentally valuable to overall happiness, which seems to get the order of explanation backwards. This objection resurfaces in Aristotle's treatment of friendship in Week 15.

Despite these four objections, utilitarianism establishes real things any adequate moral theory must reckon with: consequences matter, everyone's welfare counts equally, and morality must be practical enough to guide real decisions. What it leaves unresolved is the problem of persons: whether individuals have claims that cannot be traded off against aggregate benefit, and why. Kant's fundamental insight is that morality cannot be grounded in consequences or in any contingent feature of experience, including happiness, because doing so makes persons calculable inputs to a sum. For Kant, every rational person has a dignity that cannot be traded off against aggregate welfare at all. Understanding why requires understanding what Kant means by reason, and what he thinks reason alone can reveal about moral obligation.
