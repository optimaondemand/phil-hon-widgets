📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A ring that makes you invisible. Would you still act justly if no one could ever find out?
Socrates has just defeated Thrasymachus in Republic I. Then Glaucon, one of Plato's brothers, refuses to let the matter rest. He wants to know whether justice is genuinely good for its own sake, or only for the reputation and rewards it brings, and he is willing to argue the strongest possible case against justice to force a real answer. What follows is one of the most demanding challenges in the history of philosophy, and the rest of the Republic, and much of Module 4, is an attempt to meet it.

I will be able to explain Glaucon's three-part distinction between kinds of goods and where he thinks most people would place justice.
2

📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Plato, Republic II, 357a–367e
📚 PART 1 · THREE QUESTIONS IN ONE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Before reading further: do you think a genuinely just person would still be just even if being just cost them everything and no one ever found out? (No wrong answer.)

Glaucon opens by distinguishing three kinds of good: things good only as means (medicine, painful exercise), things good both in themselves and for their results (knowledge, health), and things good purely for their own sake (harmless pleasures). He asks Socrates where justice belongs. Socrates says the second, most admirable category. Glaucon is skeptical: most people, if honest, would put justice in the first category, something merely tolerated for its rewards. His real question is sharper than it first sounds: would a just person, believed by everyone to be unjust, still choose justice? Would an unjust person, believed by everyone to be just, regret the injustice? That is a question about whether virtue has value independent of anyone's opinion of you.

📚 PART 2 · THE RING OF GYGES

To press the point, Glaucon invents a ring that makes its wearer invisible: no fear of detection, no consequences at all. His claim is stark. Given full immunity, everyone would act purely in their own interest; the just and the unjust person do not differ in character, only in circumstance. "No one is just willingly, but only under compulsion, since justice is not a good to the individual" (Republic 360c). The ring is not a fantasy device; it is a filter. It strips away every external incentive for just behavior and asks what, if anything, is left once reputation and reward are gone.

⚡ WEIGH IT · HOW STRONG IS THE RING ARGUMENT?
Glaucon claims that no one, given full invisibility and immunity, would act justly. How strong do you find this specific claim, on its own terms? (No wrong answer, place your honest assessment.)
📚 PART 3 · THE TWO LIVES

Glaucon sharpens the challenge further by imagining two people side by side: one perfectly just but reputed a criminal, suffering every social and legal penalty of apparent injustice; the other perfectly unjust but reputed perfectly just, enjoying every reward, esteem, wealth, power, while doing exactly as he pleases. If justice were truly good in itself, the first person's life should still be worth choosing. Is that plausible? Socrates cannot simply point to the rewards of justice, because Glaucon has deliberately routed all the rewards to the unjust man. His brother Adeimantus adds a final piece: everyone who praises justice praises it for its consequences, never for what it does to the soul of the person who has it. Until someone shows that justice is worth having independent of reputation, the rational strategy looks like this: appear just, and be strategic underneath.

This is not cynicism for its own sake. Glaucon says explicitly that he wants justice to be genuinely valuable; he simply has not heard an argument that shows it. Answering him will require Socrates to develop a full theory of the soul, the city, and the good, which is the project of the entire Republic, and which Module 4 will track through Plato's own answer, then Mill's, Kant's, and Aristotle's.
