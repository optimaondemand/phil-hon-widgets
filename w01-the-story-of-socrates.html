📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A man on trial for his life defends himself, not with excuses, but with an argument for how to live.
Plato's Apology is an unusual way to begin a philosophy course. It is not a treatise or a lecture. It is a defense speech, delivered by a seventy-year-old man charged with impiety and corrupting the youth of Athens, and it is unlike any defense speech Athens had ever heard. Socrates was convicted. He was executed. And in the centuries since, his speech has been read as one of the clearest accounts ever given of what philosophy is and why it matters. This lesson tells the story of how he came to be standing in that courtroom, and what he believed about himself when he stood there.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to describe the political and religious pressures in Athens in 399 BCE that led to Socrates' trial.
2
I will be able to explain the Delphic oracle's riddle and how Socrates' investigation of it became his life's mission.
3
I will be able to interpret the gadfly image and explain what it reveals about the social function of honest questioning.

📚 PART 1 · ATHENS IN 399 BCE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Before you read further: when you hear the word “philosopher,” what image comes to mind? (No wrong answer, just check in with yourself.)

A City Under Strain

Athens in the fifth century BCE was one of the most extraordinary cities in history: one of the first democracies, home to the Parthenon, to Sophocles and Euripides, to Thucydides and Pericles. Its central marketplace, the Agora, was a place where citizens gathered not only to buy and sell but to debate and dispute. Athens prided itself on the belief that free inquiry was a civic virtue.

It was also, by 399 BCE, a city under serious strain. The late fifth century had been catastrophic: Athens fought and lost the twenty-seven-year Peloponnesian War against Sparta, leaving the city exhausted and unstable. In 404 BCE, a Spartan-backed oligarchy known as the Thirty Tyrants seized power and ruled with violence for about a year before democracy was restored. Among the young men associated with the Thirty were former companions of Socrates, most notably Critias, one of the oligarchy's ringleaders. The restored democracy had granted an amnesty for crimes committed under the Thirty, which is why Socrates' accusers could not charge him directly with political treachery. So they charged him instead with impiety and corrupting the youth.

A ROUGH TIMELINE
431–404 BCE
Peloponnesian War against Sparta; Athens loses
404–403 BCE
The Thirty Tyrants rule Athens by force
403 BCE
Democracy restored; a general amnesty is declared
399 BCE
Socrates tried on charges of impiety and corrupting the youth

The wounds from that year were still fresh. The city that tried Socrates was nervous, suspicious of intellectuals, and freshly aware of what happened when clever people with unconventional ideas gained influence over young men.

📚 PART 2 · WHO WAS SOCRATES?
Portrait of Socrates. Roman marble copy, Louvre Museum, likely after a lost bronze original by Lysippus. Photo: Eric Gaba, CC BY-SA 2.5.

Socrates was born around 470 BCE in Athens and lived his entire life there. His father was a stonecutter; his mother, according to tradition, was a midwife, and Socrates borrowed that second profession as a metaphor for his own philosophical practice. Just as a midwife does not herself give birth but helps others bring forth what is already within them, Socrates claimed not to teach but to assist others in drawing out understanding already latent within them. He called this technique maieutics, from the Greek word for midwifery.

He did not lecture. He did not found a school. He did not charge fees. He did not write treatises (the dialogues we read are by Plato, his student; Socrates himself left nothing in writing). What he did, obsessively, was talk: he walked through the streets of Athens and talked with politicians, craftsmen, poets, generals, and the young men who found his company irresistible.

What he cared about, and what made him a disruptive presence in Athens, was whether people actually knew what they claimed to know. He questioned politicians about justice, generals about courage, poets about what their poems meant. He pressed until his interlocutors were forced either to refine their thinking or admit they could not answer. Most chose to become angry. The resulting enemies, he says in the Apology, were “of the worst and most dangerous kind.” (Apology, 23a)

💡 HELPFUL TO KNOW
Socrates was famously ordinary in his material life: ugly by conventional Greek standards, indifferent to fine food and clothing, able to stand in thought for hours without moving. All of this was reported not as eccentricity but as evidence of a consistent practice. Socrates cared about the soul and its condition, not at all about the body's comforts.
📚 PART 3 · THE ORACLE AND ITS RIDDLE

Near the beginning of the Apology, Socrates explains how he came to live as he did. The story begins with his friend Chaerephon, whom he describes with mild affection as “impetuous in all his doings.” Chaerephon had gone to the oracle at Delphi, the most prestigious oracle in the Greek world, and asked whether anyone was wiser than Socrates. The Pythia, the oracle's spokesperson, replied: no one.

This reply was genuinely puzzling to Socrates. He was convinced he was not wise in any substantial sense. He could not dismiss the answer either: the oracle speaks for a god, and a god does not lie. So he began a thorough investigation to find out what the god could have meant.

He went first to the politicians, the men with the highest reputations for wisdom. He found that while they knew nothing he would call genuine wisdom, they were confidently unaware of this fact. They thought they knew. After examining them, Socrates drew a careful balance: “I am likely to be a little wiser than they are to this small extent, that I do not think I know what I do not know.” (Apology, 21d)

He went next to the poets, expecting better results. But when he questioned them about what their own beautiful lines meant, they could not explain it. They spoke well, but speaking well is not the same as understanding: “Not by wisdom do poets write poetry,” he observed, “but by a sort of genius and inspiration.” (Apology, 22c) The craftsmen were different: they knew genuine things, real and demonstrable. But because they were expert in one domain, they assumed their expertise extended to justice, to governance, to what is good for a city.

Socrates concluded that the oracle's meaning was this: human wisdom is worth very little. The person who recognizes this, who neither knows something they do not know nor believes they know something they do not, is by that slender margin wiser than those who are confidently ignorant. The oracle was not complimenting Socrates. It was using him as an illustration.

📚 PART 4 · THE PHILOSOPHER AS GADFLY

The investigation that began as a solution to a riddle became a vocation. Socrates came to understand his life's work as a divine commission: to go around Athens demonstrating that human wisdom, at its best, is a kind of organized humility.

He describes himself as a gadfly: the kind of biting fly that keeps a large horse from sleeping too soundly. Athens is the horse, great, noble, and dangerously prone to complacency. Socrates is the gadfly, stinging it awake.

“I am that gadfly which God has attached to the state, and all day long and in all places am always fastening upon you, arousing and persuading and reproaching you.” (Apology, 30e)

The image is deliberately humble. Socrates is small, annoying, and easy to swat. But he is necessary, and the horse that gets rid of the fly will sleep its way into decline. A city in which the politicians cannot define justice, the generals cannot define courage, and the poets cannot explain what they mean is a city whose confidence in itself is unearned. That gap between confidence and understanding does not disappear when no one points to it. It simply continues doing its damage in silence.

💪 HOLD ONTO THIS

Socrates did not set out to be a philosopher. He set out to solve a riddle. It was the seriousness with which he pursued the answer, testing it against politicians, poets, and craftsmen, that turned an odd errand into a way of life, and eventually into a capital charge. Keep the gadfly image in mind: philosophy's usefulness to a community is not comfort. It is friction, applied honestly.
