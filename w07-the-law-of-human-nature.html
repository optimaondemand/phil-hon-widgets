📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Listen to how people actually quarrel. It reveals something they all secretly believe.
C. S. Lewis was a literary scholar, not a professional philosopher, and a convinced atheist for the first half of his adult life. Mere Christianity began as BBC radio broadcasts during the Second World War, aimed at ordinary people living under bombardment and asking, with real urgency, whether it made sense to believe in God. Lewis begins not with abstract metaphysics but with something everyone already knows: human beings quarrel.

📚 PART 1 · AN ARGUMENT EVERYONE ALREADY KNOWS
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of the last time you said "that's not fair." Were you just reporting your own preference, or expecting the other person to recognize a standard too? (No wrong answer.)

Lewis invites us to pay attention to what people say when they quarrel: "That's my seat, I was there first"; "Leave him alone, he isn't doing you any harm"; "Give me a bit of your orange, I gave you a bit of mine." The person who says "that's my seat, I was there first" is appealing to a standard, a principle of fairness, that they believe the other person is also bound by. They are not saying "I want that seat, so give it to me." They are saying: by a rule that applies to both of us, that seat is mine.

And notice what the other person does. They rarely say "I know no such rule applies to me, and I don't care." They argue back, contesting the application of the rule: "No, I was there first." Both parties are operating within a shared framework of standards, arguing over who wins the case, not disputing whether the court has jurisdiction. This, Lewis argues, is evidence that there is a Moral Law.

📚 PART 2 · WHAT THE MORAL LAW IS NOT

Lewis distinguishes the Moral Law from two things it might be confused with. It is not a description of how people actually behave. The Moral Law tells us what we ought to do, not what we in fact do, and the gap between the two is not an embarrassment to the law; it is what makes the law what it is.

It is also not the same as instinct. When we face a moral situation, we typically experience a conflict between instincts, say, self-preservation against the pull to help a drowning stranger. Something in us evaluates which pull to follow, and that evaluating standard cannot itself be just another instinct, any more than a piano tuner is just another piano note.

⚡ TRY IT · MORAL LAW, INSTINCT, OR CONVENTION?
Classify each description using Lewis's categories.
1. The thing that tells you which of two competing urges you ought to follow
2. The felt urge to help someone in danger, before you've decided whether to act on it
📚 PART 3 · TWO OBJECTIONS, AND THE REALITY OF THE LAW

Lewis addresses two common objections. First, that the Moral Law is just social convention, since different cultures have different codes. He replies that the variation has been exaggerated ("you might just as well try to imagine a country where two and two made five"), and more importantly, that criticizing a culture's code as genuinely wrong, not merely different, presupposes a standard above any particular culture's conventions. Second, that the Moral Law is just herd instinct, evolved because it helped groups survive. Lewis grants we have such instincts but points out that we still evaluate and sometimes override our strongest impulses on moral grounds; something is doing that evaluating, and it is not another instinct.

The Moral Law is real in the sense that it is not invented by us and does not depend on our acceptance of it. If someone sincerely believes torturing innocents for entertainment is acceptable, they are not operating under a different convention; they are wrong. This puts morality in a different category from facts about the physical world: the Law of Gravity describes what things do; the Moral Law describes what things ought to do, a weight no description of actual behavior can supply on its own. In Lesson 7b, Lewis asks what this implies about the nature of the universe.
