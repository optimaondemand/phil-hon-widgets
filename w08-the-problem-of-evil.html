📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
If God is all-powerful, all-knowing, and all-good, why does evil exist at all?
There is an ancient puzzle that has haunted natural theology from its beginning, at least as old as the Greek philosopher Epicurus in the fourth century BCE. It is the most powerful challenge to everything Aquinas, Paley, and Lewis have argued this module. This week gives it the sustained attention it deserves: this lesson states the problem precisely, Lesson 8b takes up the most influential response to one kind of evil, and Lesson 8c takes up the response to the other kind, and what remains genuinely open.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to distinguish the logical problem of evil from the evidential problem.
2
I will be able to distinguish moral evil from natural evil and explain why the distinction matters for which response applies.

📚 PART 1 · THE ARGUMENT STATED
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Before this lesson, has the existence of suffering ever struck you as evidence against God, evidence that's simply unrelated to the question, or something else entirely? (No wrong answer.)

The Problem of Evil is typically presented in two related forms. The logical problem attempts to show that God's existence and evil's existence are strictly incompatible: if God is omnipotent, omniscient, and omnibenevolent, then God can, knows about, and wants to prevent every evil; therefore if God exists there is no evil; but evil exists; therefore God does not exist.

The evidential problem is weaker but more resilient: it does not claim strict contradiction, only that the amount, type, and distribution of evil in the world makes God's existence unlikely. The sheer scale of suffering, natural disasters, childhood disease, mass death in war, predation across hundreds of millions of years, seems wildly out of proportion with any purpose a good God might have, even if it is not logically impossible.

📚 PART 2 · TWO KINDS OF EVIL

Moral evil results from the free choices of rational agents: cruelty, injustice, betrayal, violence. A perfect God could have created beings who never chose evil; God did not. Natural evil is suffering from the natural order itself, earthquakes, cancer, predation, birth defects, with no human choice as its cause. A child born with a painful disease did nothing to deserve that suffering, and no human freedom explains it.

The distinction matters because the most influential response to each kind is different. The Free Will Defense, which Lesson 8b takes up in full, addresses moral evil but does not obviously extend to natural evil, since natural evil has nothing to do with human choices. The soul-making response, which Lesson 8c takes up, was developed specifically to address natural evil.
