📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A sheriff can prevent a deadly riot by executing one innocent man. The math says: do it. Something in you says: absolutely not. Which one wins?
The Principle of Utility sounds clean: do whatever produces the most happiness for the most people. The hard part is what it demands in practice, identifying everyone affected, measuring their happiness, adding it up, and comparing the total to every alternative. This lesson works through what that calculus actually requires, where it breaks down, and how rule utilitarianism tries to repair it.

📚 PART 1 · WHO COUNTS, AND THE PROBLEM OF SCALE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Before you read further: do you think it is possible, even in principle, to calculate the total happiness an action produces for everyone it affects? (No wrong answer.)

To apply the Principle of Utility, you would need to identify everyone whose happiness is affected by an action, measure how much happiness or unhappiness it produces for each of them, add up the totals, and compare the result to every alternative action. Bentham's criterion was "each to count for one, and none for more than one": a king's happiness does not outweigh a servant's. Mill endorses this and extends it, at least implicitly, to any being capable of pleasure and pain. But major decisions affect enormous numbers of people in unpredictable ways, and even ordinary personal choices ripple outward. How is anyone supposed to actually perform this calculation?

📚 PART 2 · ACT UTILITARIANISM'S DIFFICULTIES

Act utilitarianism evaluates each individual action by its actual consequences. It faces three well-known objections. The calculation problem: we rarely know the full consequences of our actions, so a theory that demands evaluating actual consequences seems to demand a foresight human beings don't have. The justice problem: a sheriff who knows that executing one innocent person will prevent a riot that kills five seems, by strict act-utilitarian logic, obligated to execute them, a conclusion most people's intuitions reject outright. The demandingness problem: if the right action always maximizes overall happiness, then almost nothing ordinary, an hour of leisure instead of donating to charity, is fully justified; the theory seems to demand constant self-sacrifice.

⚡ TRY IT · NAME THE OBJECTION
Match each scenario to the objection it illustrates.
1. A judge convicts someone they privately know is innocent, because doing so will prevent a riot that would kill five people
2. A student feels obligated to donate every spare hour and dollar to charity rather than spend any of it on friends or hobbies
📚 PART 3 · RULE UTILITARIANISM AND WHAT THE CALCULUS GETS RIGHT

Mill's text suggests a different way of applying the principle, later labeled rule utilitarianism: instead of asking what action produces the most happiness in this particular case, ask what rule, generally followed, would produce the most happiness over time, and then follow that rule even when deviating might seem to help in a single instance. Rules create reliability and trust; a society that reliably refuses to punish the innocent produces far more welfare, overall, than one that recalculates case by case. Whether rule utilitarianism is a genuine improvement, or collapses back into act utilitarianism under pressure, remains a long-standing debate: if utility is what ultimately matters, why not break the rule whenever the numbers favor it?

A deeper worry, the aggregation problem, asks whether you can really add up separate people's happiness into a single sum at all. My pain is mine; your pleasure is yours; treating five people's happiness as "outweighing" one person's suffering performs an operation with no obvious basis in anyone's actual experience. Still, despite these difficulties, the calculus establishes something real: consequences matter, everyone's welfare counts equally, and morality has to be practical enough to guide real decisions under real constraints. What it leaves unresolved is exactly the problem of persons, whether individuals have claims that cannot be traded off against aggregate welfare, and why. That is where Kant's alternative, taken up in Week 14, begins.
