📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
"It is impossible to think of anything at all in the world, or indeed even beyond it, that could be considered good without limitation except a good will."
Immanuel Kant lived his entire life in Königsberg, so regular in his habits that neighbors reportedly set their watches by his walks. In 1785 he published the Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, attempting something no one had quite tried before: to identify the supreme principle of morality through pure reason alone, without appeal to happiness, human psychology, or cultural convention. This lesson introduces why he thought that project necessary.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain Kant's claim that a good will is the only unconditional good, and why he thinks happiness is a merely conditional one.
2
I will be able to distinguish acting "in conformity with duty" from acting "from duty," and inclination from reason as sources of motivation.
3
I will be able to distinguish hypothetical from categorical imperatives and explain why moral commands must take the categorical form.

📚 PART 1 · THE GOOD WILL
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Can you think of a good quality, intelligence, courage, charm, that could still be used for something bad? (No wrong answer.)

Kant's objection to Mill is not that happiness is unimportant. It is that happiness, like health, intelligence, or friendship, is a conditional good: good when accompanied by other things (a good will, justice, proper use) and not good otherwise. A happy tyrant is not living a good life. If morality is supposed to be unconditionally binding, applying regardless of circumstance or feeling, it cannot be grounded in something conditional like happiness.

Kant opens the Groundwork with one of philosophy's most famous sentences: "It is impossible to think of anything at all in the world, or indeed even beyond it, that could be considered good without limitation except a good will." (Groundwork, 4:393) Intelligence, courage, health, even happiness, can be used well or badly and become good or bad depending on the will directing them. Only a will that acts from duty, governed by reason, is good without qualification.

📚 PART 2 · DUTY, INCLINATION, AND REASON

A will is good not because of what it produces, but because of what governs it: duty, as determined by reason. Kant distinguishes acting in conformity with duty (doing the right thing for the wrong reason, convenience, reward, feeling like it) from acting from duty (doing it because you recognize it as morally required, even at personal cost, even when no one is watching). Only the second has genuine moral worth. A merchant honest because honesty is good for business acts in conformity with duty; a person who tells a painful truth at real cost, because honesty is right, acts from duty.

This tracks a deeper distinction between inclination (our natural desires, sympathies, and impulses) and reason. Inclinations are not bad, but they are unreliable moral guides: they vary from person to person and change over time. Morality, for Kant, must be universal, applying to all rational beings alike, and only reason can generate universal principles. When reason produces a principle binding on all rational beings, it produces the moral law.

2. A shop owner is always honest with customers because a reputation for honesty is good for repeat business
📚 PART 3 · KANT'S ANSWER TO GLAUCON

Kant distinguishes two kinds of practical principle. A hypothetical imperative tells you what to do if you want some result: "if you want to be healthy, exercise." It binds only insofar as you have the relevant desire. A categorical imperative tells you what to do unconditionally, regardless of desire or circumstance: not "do not lie, if you want a good reputation," but simply, do not lie. The moral law must take this categorical form, since it has to bind every rational being regardless of what they happen to want, and Lesson 14b examines the specific formulas Kant offers for it.

Kant is answering Glaucon and Mill both. Mill's account of the good life rests on happiness, something that happens to you, an experience you have. If justice is valuable only because it makes you happy, the door stays open to a clever person pursuing the appearance of justice while actually chasing whatever maximizes happiness. Kant's account rests instead on the dignity of rational agency itself: the good will is not good because it produces happy results, but because acting from duty is the expression of what is best and most fundamental in a rational being. The unjust person, on this view, is not merely missing higher pleasures or producing aggregate unhappiness; they are failing to live in accordance with their own nature as a rational being, failing to respect the moral law in themselves and in others.

💪 HOLD ONTO THIS

Kant is not asking you to distrust your feelings. He is asking you to notice that feelings come and go, and morality, if it is real, cannot come and go with them.
