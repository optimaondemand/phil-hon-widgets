📚 PART 1 · SOUL-MAKING
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of a hard experience that actually made you more courageous or more compassionate. Does that make the difficulty feel justified, or does it feel like a separate question? (No wrong answer.)

The most influential response to natural evil appeals to soul-making, or the Irenaean theodicy, associated with the early Christian theologian Irenaeus and developed philosophically by John Hick in the twentieth century. The idea: God did not create human beings as finished, perfect beings but as creatures in a process of moral and spiritual development. The purpose of human existence is not comfortable happiness but growth: the development of courage, compassion, endurance, and wisdom. These virtues cannot develop in an environment of perfect safety. Courage requires genuine danger; compassion requires genuine suffering to respond to. A world of total security would be a world in which these capacities could never develop. On this view, natural evil is not a sign of divine indifference but of intention: the suffering involved is regrettable but necessary, the way the difficulty of training is regrettable but necessary for an athlete's development.

📚 PART 2 · THE SHARPEST OBJECTION

The most searching objection is that the amount of suffering in the world seems vastly out of proportion with what would be needed to develop virtue. A child dying of cancer at four is not developing virtue through that suffering; she is simply suffering. The death of hundreds of thousands in a tsunami does not appear to be part of any reasonable program of spiritual education for the individuals killed. Soul-making, critics argue, can justify some suffering, but not the particular distribution and scale the world actually contains.

📚 PART 3 · LEWIS'S COUNTER-MOVE

Lewis addresses the Problem of Evil directly and acknowledges it was the argument that kept him from belief during his years as an atheist: "My argument against God was that the universe seemed so cruel and unjust." But he noticed something he considered decisive. His argument depended on a sense that the universe was unjust, a standard of justice by which he judged the universe inadequate. Where did that standard come from? If the universe is purely material, there is no standard of justice that applies to it at all; events simply happen. To call the universe cruel requires a standard of cruelty, and cruelty is a moral category that only makes sense if there is a Moral Law.

"Thus in the very act of trying to prove that God did not exist… I found I was forced to assume that one part of reality, namely my idea of justice, was full of sense." This does not resolve the Problem of Evil; Lewis is not arguing suffering is actually good. He is arguing that the objection, stated as an argument against God, is philosophically unstable: it borrows its normative force from the very framework it is trying to undermine. A more careful version of the argument can avoid this trap by not calling the universe unjust, only by saying the world looks different from what we would expect if a perfectly good, all-powerful God existed. That more careful version survives Lewis's counter-move.

📚 PART 4 · WHAT REMAINS GENUINELY OPEN

You have now read four of the most important texts in the Western debate about God: Aquinas's Cosmological Arguments, Paley's Design Argument, Hume's skeptical critique, Lewis's Moral Argument, and the Problem of Evil in both its logical and evidential forms. Intellectual honesty requires saying plainly that none of the responses examined this week, the Free Will Defense, soul-making, Lewis's counter-move, fully resolves the evidential problem. They address important dimensions of it. They do not make it disappear. What they show is that the Problem of Evil, while formidable, is not obviously decisive either. Both sides of this question have genuine philosophical work left to do, and serious thinkers, theists and non-theists, continue to do it.

This is where Module 2 leaves the question of God: genuinely open, with real arguments on both sides that deserve to be taken seriously rather than dismissed. If there is a necessary, intelligent, morally authoritative source behind the universe, a natural question follows: what kind of beings are we, that we can ask these questions at all, sense the pull of the Moral Law, and fear or hope about what happens when we die? That is the question of the soul, and it is where Module 3 begins.
