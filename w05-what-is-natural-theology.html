📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What can reason alone, without scripture, tell us about God?
Module 2 turns from the method of philosophy to one of its oldest applications. Over the next four weeks you will read a medieval theologian who believed reason and faith point to the same truth, a natural historian who found evidence of God in the structure of the eye, a Scottish philosopher who doubted reason could settle the matter, and a twentieth-century author who argued morality itself is the best evidence for God. This lesson asks the question that has to come first: what is natural theology, and what is it trying to do?

📚 PART 1 · TWO WAYS OF KNOWING ABOUT GOD
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Before this lesson, if someone asked you "how would you know whether God exists?", would your answer have leaned on reason, on a religious text or tradition, or on personal experience? (No wrong answer.)

The Western tradition distinguishes two ways of knowing about God. Revealed theology is knowledge derived from sacred scripture, prophetic tradition, or the direct self-disclosure of God. It depends on the testimony of witnesses and the authority of a tradition. Natural theology is knowledge derived from natural reason operating on the evidence of the natural world. It is available, in principle, to any careful thinker regardless of religious tradition. It does not begin with "God has revealed," but with "what can we infer from what we observe?"

The distinction matters for what natural theology can and cannot do. It cannot establish the specific doctrines of any religion, that Jesus rose from the dead, that Muhammad received the Quran from Gabriel, that God made a covenant with the Jewish people. What natural theology aims to establish is more modest: that there is some kind of God, some being that is ultimate, self-sufficient, and responsible for the existence and order of everything else. Whether it succeeds is the question this module examines.

📚 PART 2 · ARISTOTLE'S UNMOVED MOVER

Long before the term "natural theology" existed, Aristotle argued from the existence of motion and change to something he called the Unmoved Mover: a being that causes all motion without itself being moved. Everything that moves is moved by something else. The stone is moved by the hand; the hand by the muscles; the muscles by the will. This chain cannot go back forever: if there is no first mover that is not itself moved by anything prior, the whole chain of motion never gets started. But motion evidently exists. Therefore there must be a first mover that is itself unmoved.

This Unmoved Mover does not make covenants, answer prayers, or deliver commandments. It is more like a pure act of thinking, eternal and necessary. Aristotle's argument was written as physics and metaphysics, not theology. But medieval scholars who encountered his works through Arabic translations in the twelfth century recognized that his Unmoved Mover bore a strong resemblance to the God of monotheism, a resemblance you will be equipped to evaluate by the end of this module.

📚 PART 3 · WHAT NATURAL THEOLOGY IS NOT

Natural theology is not the same as religion. It requires no prayer, worship, ritual, or membership in a tradition. Its conclusions, if valid, would be accessible to anyone capable of careful reasoning. It is also not the same as "proving God exists" in a way that would compel a person unwilling to engage seriously; arguments only work when both parties reason in good faith. And it is not in competition with faith, at least not for its major practitioners: Thomas Aquinas believed firmly in revelation and thought natural theology confirmed that faith was reasonable, not that it replaced faith.

Sort each claim by which kind of theology it belongs to.

📚 PART 4 · WHY THIS MATTERS FOR PHILOSOPHY

You might wonder why a course organized around Plato's dialogues arrives, in its fifth week, at the question of God's existence. The question of how to live cannot be fully answered without some account of what kind of world we live in. If the universe is the product of intelligence and intention, the examined life is answerable to something beyond itself. If the universe is merely a vast arrangement of matter indifferent to human striving, the question of how to live has a different character altogether.

Plato took these questions seriously. In the Timaeus he argues the universe was fashioned by a divine craftsman. In the Phaedo, Socrates approaches his death with confidence in the goodness of the divine order. In the Republic, the Form of the Good is described in terms many later readers identified with God. The question of natural theology was latent in Plato from the beginning. Lesson 5b turns to the thinker who translated Aristotle's philosophical inheritance into a systematic natural theology: Thomas Aquinas.
