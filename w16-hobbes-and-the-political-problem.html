📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why can't people just live together without a government?
Every question so far this course has been about a single person: what can I know, does God exist, what is my soul, what is the good life for me. Module 5 turns the lens outward: what is the good life for us, together? Our entry point is Thomas Hobbes, who lived through the English Civil War and concluded that the deepest political question is not how to achieve the best community, but how to prevent the worst.

2

📚 PART 1 · WHO IS HOBBES?
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Do you think people would cooperate peacefully with each other if there were no laws, police, or government at all? (No wrong answer.)

Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679) lived through the English Civil War, in which King Charles I was executed and the country was plunged into armed conflict. He had seen political authority collapse, and he wanted to understand why it was necessary. His masterwork, Leviathan (1651), takes its title from the biblical sea monster: an artificial man, immense and powerful, created by human beings to protect them from one another.

Hobbes's method was distinctive. Where Plato and Aristotle began with teleology, questions about human beings' natural ends, Hobbes began with mechanism, influenced by the new science of Galileo and Descartes. Human beings, on his account, are not naturally drawn toward the good; they are propelled by desires and aversions like matter in motion.

📚 PART 2 · THE PROBLEM HOBBES IS SOLVING

Most of us take political order for granted: laws, police, courts, a government that enforces rules. We might disagree about whether specific laws are just, but we generally assume some political order is necessary. Hobbes asks why. Why can't people just cooperate peacefully without an authority to compel them? His answer is that human nature makes peaceful cooperation without authority inherently unstable, not because people are exceptionally evil, but because of structural features of the human condition. To see why, he asks us to imagine human beings stripped of every institution, no law, no government, no police, and ask what life would be like. That imaginary condition is the state of nature, examined in Lesson 16b.

⚡ TRY IT · TELEOLOGY OR MECHANISM?
Classify each approach to explaining human political life.
1. Human beings act because desires and aversions push and pull them, the way physical forces move matter, with no final resting point
2. Human beings act because all activity aims at a final end, eudaimonia, toward which a complete human life is oriented
📚 PART 3 · NO FINAL SATISFACTION

Hobbes's account of human motivation is strikingly modern. "Felicity" is not a resting point but a continual forward motion: "the felicity of this life consisteth not in the repose of a mind satisfied... but in a continual progress of the desire, from one object to another; the attaining of the former being still but the way to the latter." (Leviathan, ch. 11) This directly rejects Aristotle's claim that human beings have a final end, eudaimonia, at which a complete life arrives. Hobbes says there is no final satisfaction; one desire fulfilled simply makes room for the next.

This has a direct political implication. If there is no natural final end human beings converge on, there is no natural political order that springs from a shared vision of the good. We cannot organize society around agreement about the highest good, because there is no such agreement. Politics, for Hobbes, must be organized around something more minimal: preventing the worst.

💪 HOLD ONTO THIS

Hobbes is not arguing that people are wicked. He is arguing that even decent people, with no shared vision of the good and no authority above them, produce disaster together. That is a very different, and in some ways more unsettling, claim.
