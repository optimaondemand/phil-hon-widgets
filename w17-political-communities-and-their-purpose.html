📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
"The polis comes into being for the sake of living, but it exists for the sake of the good life."
Aristotle traces a progression from household, to village, to polis, each association purposive, existing for the sake of some good. Understanding what each level is for reveals the heart of Aristotle's political philosophy, and its direct disagreement with Hobbes.

📚 PART 1 · FROM HOUSEHOLD TO VILLAGE TO POLIS
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Do you think a government's job should be limited to keeping people safe, or should it also actively promote things like community, culture, and shared values? (No wrong answer.)

The Politics opens: "Every polis is a community of some kind, and every community is established with a view to some good." (Politics I.1, 1252a1–3) Aristotle traces a progression. The household (oikos) is the most basic community, existing for daily life, reproduction, and sustenance; it is governed by necessity. Several households combine into a village, governed like an extended family and meeting more than daily needs, long-term security, trade, shared religious practice, but it is still not self-sufficient. When several villages unite into a community large enough to be self-sufficient, the polis comes into being.

Note how this differs from Hobbes, who begins with isolated individuals and must explain how they could ever agree to live together. For Aristotle, the isolated individual is the abstraction; the household, an inherently communal unit, is the given.

📚 PART 2 · THE POLIS EXISTS FOR THE GOOD LIFE

Aristotle's key sentence: "The polis comes into being for the sake of living, but it exists for the sake of the good life." (Politics I.2, 1252b29–30) This is directly opposed to Hobbes. Hobbes says the state exists for security, to prevent violent death. Aristotle says: yes, the polis comes into being for survival, but its real purpose, the measure by which it should be judged, is the good life, eudaimonia. A polis is not a large household or mutual defense pact; it is the community in which human beings can deliberate together about justice and provide one another the conditions for flourishing.

📚 PART 3 · WHAT THE POLIS MAKES POSSIBLE, AND A HARD TRUTH ABOUT ARISTOTLE

A city of sufficient size supports philosophical life (the leisure and community needed for inquiry), political life (deliberating, governing, and being governed in turn), artistic and cultural life, and legal life. None of these are possible for an isolated individual or a small village; they require a community of sufficient size and stability. Aristotle closes this section: "Justice is the bond of men in states; for the administration of justice, which is the determination of what is just, is the principle of order in political society." (Politics I.2, 1253a37–38) Justice, for Aristotle as for Plato, is not merely legal rules or a social contract; it is the organizing principle of a genuine political community.

It must be said plainly: Aristotle also argued in Politics I.4–7 that slavery is natural, that some people are by nature suited to be slaves. This argument is wrong, and its premise, that some people are so deficient in practical reason they are better governed by others, would not in any case justify slavery as it was actually practiced, which enslaved people by conquest and birth, not by any assessment of rational capacity. Aristotle's teleological claim that the polis exists for the sake of the good life does not depend on this indefensible view; it stands on its own and deserves engagement on its merits, while his views on slavery deserve unambiguous rejection.
