📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Say there is only one substance, not two. The problem does not disappear. It changes shape.
The interaction problem leaves dualism in a vulnerable position. The natural response is materialism (or physicalism): everything, including the mind, is ultimately physical. This seems like the commonsense scientific view. But once philosophers try to work it out precisely, they run into difficulties nearly as serious as the ones they were trying to escape.

📚 PART 1 · THREE FORMS OF MATERIALISM
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Do you think a computer could ever genuinely feel something, the way you feel pain, or only ever simulate feeling it? (No wrong answer.)

Identity theory: mental states are identical to brain states; your pain is C-fiber firing. This avoids the interaction problem entirely, but if pain just is C-fiber firing, no creature without C-fibers, an octopus, a robot, could feel pain, which seems wrong.

Functionalism refines this: pain is whatever functional state plays the "pain role," regardless of what physically implements it, allowing mental states to be multiply realized in neurons or silicon alike. But consider a philosophical zombie, functionally identical to you in every way, with nothing it is like to be it. If that is even possible, functionalism has left out exactly what is most puzzling about the mind.

Eliminative materialism is more radical still: our ordinary mental vocabulary, beliefs, desires, is a folk theory as false as phlogiston, and mature neuroscience will eliminate it rather than reduce it. Critics note this seems self-undermining: if there are no beliefs, there is no belief that eliminative materialism is true.

⚡ TRY IT · WHICH VIEW, AND WHICH PROBLEM?
Match the view to the objection that targets it specifically.
1. "This view can't explain how an octopus, with a totally different nervous system, could feel pain the way we do."
2. "Asserting this view requires having beliefs, but the view itself says there are no beliefs."
📚 PART 2 · THE HARD PROBLEM

Philosopher David Chalmers distinguishes the easy problems of consciousness, explaining integration, attention, and behavioral report, which are difficult but tractable in principle, from the hard problem: why is there subjective experience at all? "Why is it that when our cognitive systems engage in visual and auditory information-processing, we have visual or auditory experience?" (Chalmers, 1996) Even a complete physical description of the brain seems logically compatible with there being no experience at all. That gap between the physical and the experiential is what no current form of materialism has bridged.

For this course, the key takeaway is not which view is correct, philosophers have not settled that, but why the problem is so hard: the mind seems to be two things at once, a physical object that is also a subject of experience. You have now spent two weeks on a single question: what kind of thing are you? Plato's Phaedo gave one answer, an immortal soul temporarily housed in a mortal body. Descartes gave a second, a thinking substance united with an extended substance in ways that remain mysterious. Modern materialism gives a family of answers, each handling some aspects and struggling with others. Next week returns to Plato, to Republic IV, for a third picture, and asks how all three fit together.
