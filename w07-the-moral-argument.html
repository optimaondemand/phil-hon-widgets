📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
If there is a real "ought" in the universe, what kind of universe has room for one?
Lesson 7a established that there is a Moral Law: a real standard human beings recognize as binding, not reducible to instinct or custom. Now Lewis asks what this implies about the universe. This is the move that turns the Moral Argument into a piece of natural theology rather than merely moral philosophy.

📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
C. S. Lewis, Mere Christianity, Book I, ch. 4
📚 PART 1 · TWO VIEWS OF THE UNIVERSE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN · BEFORE YOU READ
Right now, how strongly do you lean toward the idea that the universe is fundamentally purposeless matter, versus fundamentally the product of mind and intention? (No wrong answer, just notice where you land.)

Lewis frames the question between two rival accounts. The materialist view: the universe is matter and energy operating according to physical laws, without directing intelligence or purpose. What we call laws of nature are regularities, not norms; nothing about them says what ought to happen. The religious view (a family of views, not one specific religion): the universe was made by a power or mind that had intentions and values behind it. Lewis does not argue for the second view by pointing to biological complexity, as Paley did. He argues from the existence of the Moral Law.

📚 PART 2 · FROM MORAL LAW TO MORAL MIND

The Moral Law is not a physical fact; it is a standard, telling us how we ought to behave. No description of how things in fact are can, by itself, generate an "ought." The only things we know from direct experience that can issue commands, set standards, and hold others accountable are minds. "We want to know whether the universe simply happens to be what it is for no reason, or whether there is a power behind it… a power that is strongly interested in right conduct." (Mere Christianity, Book I, ch. 4) A material universe has regularities but no obligations, causes but no norms. The existence of the Moral Law is best explained by something with the character of a mind behind the universe, which Lewis, following the tradition, calls God.

THE ARGUMENT'S STRUCTURE
1. There is a real Moral Law, objectively binding, not reducible to instinct, custom, or physical fact.
2. Real laws are the products of lawgivers.
3. The Moral Law was not made by any human being or institution.
4. Therefore it was made by something non-human.
5. A lawgiver capable of setting an objective moral standard for all human beings must be something like a mind.
6. This is what the tradition calls God.

This differs from Aquinas's and Paley's starting points. The Cosmological Arguments begin from existence; the Design Argument begins from order and purpose; the Moral Argument begins from value and obligation.

📚 PART 3 · HUME AND LEWIS: A COMPARISON

Hume's response to Paley was that the analogy between organisms and artifacts is too weak. His likely response to Lewis would target a different premise: why think laws require lawgivers at all? Physical laws are not issued by anyone; the laws of thermodynamics describe regularities without any mind behind them. Perhaps moral regularities are similar: patterns in how beings with our nature evaluate conduct, without any mind that issued the standard.

⚡ TRY IT · WHOSE MOVE IS IT?
Match the move to the philosopher most likely to make it.
1. "Physical laws describe what happens; moral laws describe what ought to happen, and that 'ought' cannot be derived from mere description."
2. "The laws of thermodynamics were not issued by anyone, so maybe moral regularities do not require a mind either."

This is a genuine philosophical dispute, and it is not closed. Lewis's argument depends on the reality of the "ought," on moral obligations being facts about what beings like us are actually required to do, not simply facts about psychology or evolution.

📚 PART 4 · LEWIS'S OWN CONCLUSION

Lewis is careful not to overstate his argument. It shows, if it succeeds, that something behind the universe has the character of a mind concerned with right conduct. It does not show that this something is the personal God of Christian revelation. What the Moral Argument does show, if Lewis is right, is that materialism is false: the universe is not simply matter in motion, indifferent to human value. And there is an uncomfortable corollary: if the Moral Law is real and holds us accountable, it is a fact about us, about what we are required to do and how far we fall short. Lesson 7c would ordinarily take up the most formidable challenge to everything this module has argued: if there is a good, powerful God, why does evil exist? That question gets a full module of its own in Week 8.
