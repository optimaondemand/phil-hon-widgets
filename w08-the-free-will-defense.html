📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A world of puppets who always choose well, or a world of free beings who sometimes choose badly. Which is better?
The most influential response to moral evil is the Free Will Defense, developed in its contemporary philosophical form by Alvin Plantinga but anticipated much earlier in the Christian tradition. This lesson lays out the argument carefully, and takes its real limitations just as seriously as its force.

📚 PART 1 · THE ARGUMENT
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Would a world where people were mechanically compelled to always act kindly be better, or worse, than our actual world? (No wrong answer.)

The argument runs like this. Genuine freedom, the capacity to choose between real alternatives, including real moral alternatives, is a good of a very high order. A world containing beings who freely choose the good is, all else equal, better than one containing beings mechanically compelled to perform good acts. God, in creating free beings, chose to create a world where the good could be freely chosen, and this entails that evil could also be freely chosen, since freedom without the genuine possibility of evil is not genuine freedom.

The suffering caused by human choices, war, cruelty, injustice, is therefore not evidence against a good God. It is the cost of creating free beings capable of genuine love, virtue, and moral achievement. God could have prevented moral evil by creating beings without freedom, but that would be a lesser creation: a world of puppets.

📚 PART 2 · OBJECTIONS

The Free Will Defense does not settle the question entirely. Philosophers have pressed several objections: could God not have created beings who, while genuinely free, happened always to choose the good? Why give human beings such a robust capacity for destructive evil, not merely for minor self-indulgence? And most pressingly: the Free Will Defense does not explain natural evil at all. Earthquakes and childhood cancer are not produced by human choices, and no appeal to freedom touches them.

⚡ TRY IT · DOES THE FREE WILL DEFENSE ANSWER IT?
For each case, decide whether the Free Will Defense is even a candidate response.
1. A hurricane destroys a coastal village, killing people who had no warning
2. A soldier chooses to commit a war crime against civilians

This gap is exactly why the Problem of Evil needs a second response. Lesson 8c takes up the theodicy developed specifically to address natural evil.
