Lesson 3a
The Problem of Definition: Meno's Search for Virtue
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Can virtue be taught? Before that question can be answered, a harder one has to be: what is virtue?
Meno, a young Thessalian who has studied with the Sophist Gorgias, puts the question to Socrates without preamble. He expects a direct reply. Socrates obliges him, but not in the way he expects: “I confess, Meno, to my shame, that I know literally nothing about virtue; and when I do not know the ‘what' of anything, how can I know the ‘what sort'?” (Meno, 71a–b) Before we can ask whether virtue can be taught, we need to know what virtue is, and this lesson shows how hard that question turns out to be.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain why a list of virtues is not the same as a definition of virtue.
2
I will be able to trace why Meno's three attempted definitions of virtue each fail.
3
I will be able to explain the torpedo fish comparison and what it reveals about the state Meno is left in.

Plato's Meno, assigned excerpt: “Meno's List and the Bee”
📚 PART 1 · WHO IS MENO?
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
If someone asked you right now, "what is virtue?", how quickly could you answer? (No wrong answer.)

Meno is not a comic figure. He is educated, confident, and articulate, a man whose confidence is genuine and whose ignorance is therefore all the more instructive. He is, in this respect, very like Euthyphro. The historical Meno of Thessaly was also, by most ancient accounts, a mercenary commander of questionable character; the man who opens a dialogue about virtue by claiming to know what it is will turn out to have a reputation for very little of it himself. Whether or not students know this history, the point stands on its own: the confidence with which Meno approaches the question is the first thing that will be shaken.

📚 PART 2 · MENO'S FIRST ANSWER: A LIST

When Socrates asks what virtue is, Meno gives the most natural kind of answer: a man's virtue is to administer the state well and help friends while harming enemies; a woman's virtue is to manage the household and obey her husband. Every age and condition of life has a different virtue: “there are virtues numberless… for virtue is relative to the actions and ages of each of us in all that we do.” (Meno, 71e–72a)

Meno has given a list of virtues, not a definition of virtue. Socrates reaches for an analogy: if someone asked what a bee is, and you replied by listing kinds of bees, the honeybee, the bumblebee, the carpenter bee, you would not have answered the question. You would have identified instances without saying what they all have in common as bees. “I want to ask you not to think that you have given me an account of virtue,” Socrates says. “I want to know about this very virtue itself, the virtue that is present in all of these.” (Meno, 72c, paraphrased) The demand is identical to what we saw in the Euthyphro: an essence, not a list.

💡 WHAT MAKES THIS HARDER THAN THE EUTHYPHRO
Piety is one virtue among several; virtue is the whole category. To define virtue, you need to know not just what courage is or what justice is, but what courage, justice, temperance, and wisdom all have in common. A soldier's courage and a mother's patience look very different, exercised in different circumstances, involving different emotions and skills. Are they instances of the same thing? If so, what is that thing?
📚 PART 3 · MENO'S SECOND AND THIRD ATTEMPTS

Meno's second attempt is that virtue is the power of governing men. This fails the same way as the first: it applies only to people with authority over others, and does not cover the virtue of children, servants, or a slave, who has no one to govern, even though we readily call slaves virtuous or vicious.

His third attempt is more sophisticated: virtue is the desire for beautiful things and the ability to attain them. Socrates asks whether the desire must be for genuinely good things, or merely for what one believes to be good. If the latter, then someone who desires and attains something bad, power gained through injustice, would count as virtuous, which seems wrong. If the former, the definition collapses: everyone desires what they take to be good, so the distinguishing feature of virtue must lie in the nature of what is desired, not in the mere having of the desire.

By now Meno is in difficulty. He makes a comparison that has become famous: he says Socrates is like a torpedo fish, a ray that numbs whatever touches it. Before meeting Socrates he had spoken eloquently about virtue many times; now he cannot even say what it is. Socrates accepts the image, with one qualification: the torpedo fish numbs others because it is itself numb. “I perplex others, not because I am clear, but because I am utterly perplexed.” (Meno, 80c)

⚡ TRY IT · WHY DID IT FAIL?
Match each of Meno's attempts to the specific reason it did not survive.
1. Attempt 1: separate virtues for men, women, children, and slaves
2. Attempt 2: virtue is the power of governing men
3. Attempt 3: virtue is desiring beautiful things and being able to attain them
