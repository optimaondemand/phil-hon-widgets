📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
If the argument is as strong as Cleanthes believes, it should survive being tested. Philo tests it.
Cleanthes has made his case: the universe resembles a machine, machines have designers, therefore the universe has a designer. Philo does not dismiss the inference; he respects it enough to press it hard, through Parts II and III. This lesson reconstructs his main objections one at a time.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to reconstruct Philo's four main objections to the Design Argument.
2
I will be able to explain Cleanthes' Part III reply to Philo's skepticism.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Hume, Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion, assigned excerpt: “Philo's Objections”
📚 PART 1 · WE HAVE ONLY ONE UNIVERSE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
If you had only ever seen one example of something in your entire life, how confident would you feel generalizing about how it came to be? (No wrong answer.)

When Paley infers a watchmaker from a watch, the inference is backed by vast experience: we know how watches come to be because we have seen many made. We have experience of exactly one universe, and have never observed another universe being made. "Have worlds ever been formed under your eye," Philo asks, "and have you had leisure to observe the whole progress of the phenomenon?" (Dialogues, Part II) Our principle that purposive organization implies a designer was derived from experience with human artifacts; whether it applies to universes is precisely the question at issue, and our experience does not reach that far.

📚 PART 2 · THREE MORE OBJECTIONS

Even granting some inference is possible, Philo argues the analogy between the universe and a human artifact is too weak to carry Cleanthes' conclusion. A watch is discrete mechanical parts, separately manufactured and assembled; the universe is a continuous natural whole. The more carefully we examine the resemblance, the less it holds.

Cleanthes' argument is supposed to establish one God, but if we take the artifact analogy seriously, all the large projects we know of, shipbuilding, cathedral construction, are the work of many minds, not one. "Many worlds might have been botched and bungled, throughout an eternity, ere this system was struck out." (Dialogues, Part V) The analogy, pressed consistently, licenses a much messier range of conclusions than Cleanthes wants: a committee of designers, or an imperfect one still learning the craft.

Finally, Philo asks why we should assume only mind can produce order at all. Vegetation grows in regular patterns; crystals form in precise geometrical shapes; these processes occur with no observable intelligence directing them. "What peculiar privilege has this little agitation of the brain which we call ‘thought,' that we must thus make it the model of the whole universe?" (Dialogues, Part II) This is not Darwin's argument; Philo does not know about natural selection. It is a more general point: our inclination to explain order by invoking mind might be a cognitive bias, since minds are the most familiar source of designed objects in our own experience, not a reliable universal rule.

⚡ TRY IT · MATCH THE OBJECTION
Match each summary to Philo's specific objection.
1. "Great human artifacts are usually built by teams, not solitary geniuses."
2. "Crystals and rivers form regular, orderly patterns without any mind directing them."
📚 PART 3 · WHAT PHILO IS NOT SAYING, AND CLEANTHES' REPLY

It is important to be precise here: Philo is not arguing that God does not exist. He is arguing that the Design Argument, in Cleanthes' form, does not establish that God exists. An argument can fail without its conclusion being false. This is a characteristic Humean move: identify and maintain genuine uncertainty rather than trade one confident claim for an equally confident claim in the opposite direction.

Cleanthes does not yield. In Part III he argues Philo is applying an unreasonably high standard: we accept causal inferences from single, striking cases all the time, inferring that the Romans built a road from the road's own structure, without a wide sample of comparable roads. The order of the universe, Cleanthes argues, is sufficiently striking to justify the inference even without a wide sample of universes. Lesson 6c takes up how this debate develops in Part IV, where Demea enters more fully and the three parties find themselves in unexpected alignments.
