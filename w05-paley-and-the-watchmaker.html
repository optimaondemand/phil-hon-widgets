📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A stone on a heath needs no explanation. A watch does. Why?
In 1802, William Paley published Natural Theology, arguing that the natural world itself, its plants, animals, and physical mechanisms, supplies conclusive evidence for an intelligent Creator. The book opens with one of the most famous thought experiments in philosophy: the watchmaker analogy.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain the watch/stone distinction and why it forces an inference to design.
2
I will be able to state the formal structure of the argument from design and identify that it argues by analogy.
3
I will be able to explain why Darwin's theory of natural selection specifically threatens Paley's version of the argument.

📚 PART 1 · THE WATCH ON THE HEATH
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
If you found a smartphone on an uninhabited island, how confident would you be that someone made it, versus that it just appeared? (No wrong answer.)

Suppose you are crossing a heath and your foot strikes a stone. You would likely feel no particular need for a deep explanation; the stone could have simply always been there. Now suppose your foot strikes a watch. "I should hardly think of the answer I had before given." (Natural Theology, ch. 1) Why is the watch different? When you examine it, you find a spring, wheels, a dial, all fitted together in a very particular way so the hands move to indicate time. Every part serves a function in a system designed to accomplish a purpose. The stone's parts, if it even has parts, are not arranged to serve any function.

The watch, Paley argues, compels an inference: "there must have existed, at some time, and at some place or other, an artificer or artificers, who formed it for the purpose we find it to serve." The inference is not merely probable; Paley thinks it is as strong as any inference we are entitled to draw about the origin of anything.

⚡ TRY IT · STONE OR WATCH?
For each feature, decide whether it points to "just there, no designer needed" (stone) or "purposively organized, needs a designer" (watch).
1. A lens that focuses light precisely onto a light-sensitive surface
2. An irregularly shaped pebble worn smooth by a river over time
📚 PART 2 · THREE OBJECTIONS PALEY ANTICIPATES

Paley considers three ways a skeptic might resist the inference, and each is worth knowing because Hume will press exactly these pressure points. First: what if we had never seen a watch before? Paley says the inference is grounded in the watch's structure, not our prior knowledge of watchmaking. Second: what if the watch worked imperfectly? Even a defective watch is clearly the product of design; imperfection qualifies the inference, it does not erase it. Third: what if we simply do not know how the watch came to be? Ignorance of the details does not license doubt about whether a cause exists at all.

These anticipatory replies reveal Paley as a careful arguer who knows the objections and addresses them before his opponent can raise them. You will see in Week 6 how David Hume, writing before Paley, had already pushed much harder on these same pressure points than Paley's replies acknowledge.

📚 PART 3 · THE ANALOGY EXTENDED: THE EYE

Paley's favorite example is the eye: a transparent cornea, a lens that focuses light, a retina that converts light into signals the nervous system can interpret, each part precisely fitted to its function. "Were there no example in the world of contrivance except that of the eye, it would be alone sufficient to support the conclusion which we draw from it." (Natural Theology, ch. 3)

What Paley is offering is an argument from design, or teleological argument (telos, Greek for purpose): complex, purposively organized objects, when we encounter them anywhere else, are invariably the product of intelligent design; the natural world contains such objects; therefore the natural world is the product of intelligent design, by a designer supremely intelligent given the scale of what is designed. The argument proceeds by analogy: the natural world is like a watch; watches require watchmakers; therefore the natural world requires something like a watchmaker, only vastly more capable. How strong that analogy is, and whether it survives the differences between a watch and a living organism, is precisely what Hume presses in the Dialogues.

📚 PART 4 · DARWIN'S CHALLENGE

Paley's argument is closely related to Aquinas's Fifth Way, but it is more empirical and analogical, which makes it more vivid, and also more vulnerable. Because Paley's argument depends on analogy, any theory that explains the appearance of design without invoking a designer strikes directly at his premise. That theory arrived in 1859, when Charles Darwin argued that natural selection could produce the appearance of design without any designer at all: the eye is not planned by a craftsman but the accumulated result of millions of small changes, each conferring a survival advantage.

Darwin had read Natural Theology carefully as a young man and admired its rigor; he understood exactly what he was trying to explain, and that his theory, if correct, deprived Paley's analogy of its force. Whether Darwin's success against Paley also undermines Aquinas is a harder question, one that depends on whether Aquinas's argument stands on metaphysical ground evolutionary biology simply does not reach. That debate is still alive.
