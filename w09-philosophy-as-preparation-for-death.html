📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Socrates's friends expected grief in that prison cell. They found a man doing philosophy.
Plato's Phaedo is the account of Socrates's final conversation, on the day the Athenian authorities administered the hemlock. Its central claim is provocative enough to stop a reader cold: the philosopher should not merely accept death but welcome it, because the philosopher has been practicing death all along. This lesson introduces the dialogue and the argument behind that claim.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to state Socrates's definition of death and explain what it assumes about the soul.
2
I will be able to explain what Socrates means by calling philosophy "the practice of dying."
3
I will be able to distinguish the soul from the body as Socrates uses those terms in the Phaedo.

📚 PART 1 · THE SETTING
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
If you knew you had one final conversation with people you loved, what would you want that conversation to be about? (No wrong answer.)

The dialogue opens with a frame: Echecrates, living too far from Athens to have been present, asks Phaedo of Elis, one of those who was there, to tell him everything. The account of Socrates's last day is transmitted from Phaedo, to Echecrates, to Plato's dialogue, to us; the dialogue is itself an act of philosophical memory. Among those present: Crito, Simmias and Cebes of Thebes, and Socrates's wife Xanthippe, sent away early because she cannot stop weeping. Socrates, by contrast, is calm, and has been composing poetry during his final days. When Cebes asks why, Socrates takes the question seriously, and that seriousness opens the conversation that occupies the rest of the dialogue.

📚 PART 2 · DEFINING DEATH

Before Socrates can defend his attitude toward death, he needs to say what death is: a characteristically philosophical move, since before evaluating whether something is good or bad, you need to understand what it is. Socrates's definition is precise: death is the separation of the soul from the body. When a person dies, the soul, the living, thinking, knowing part, departs from the body. What remains is the corpse: matter without life, without thought, without the person.

This assumes a distinction Socrates develops carefully: a human being is not simply a body, and not simply a soul, but a soul currently joined to a body. The joining is temporary. The immediate consequence is that dying is not the destruction of the person but the departure of the person from the body. Whether that departure is good or bad depends on what the soul is and where it goes, which is what the rest of the Phaedo examines.

📚 PART 3 · PHILOSOPHY AS THE PRACTICE OF DYING

With death defined, Socrates makes his central claim, sharply: "Those who pursue philosophy aright are practicing nothing but dying and being dead" (Phaedo 64a). The body, in his account, is a constant source of distraction for the soul, pulling it toward pleasure and physical gratification rather than truth. The senses in particular are a problem: they deal in appearances and approximations, never in the precise reality behind them. Philosophy is the attempt to get beyond appearances by withdrawing attention from the senses and directing the mind toward pure thought.

To philosophize well is therefore to do what death literally does: separate the soul from the body. "And what is purification but the separation of the soul from the body as far as possible…?" (Phaedo 67c–d) The philosopher who has spent a life in this practice has been rehearsing, in a limited way, what death will accomplish completely. This is why Socrates should welcome death rather than fear it, and why he maintains his composure in front of his grieving friends: not indifference to them, but the settled result of understanding what is about to happen and why it is not bad.
